Scenario Governance

Scenario governance defines how scenarios are created, reviewed, approved, and promoted. Without it, the planning environment fills with stale or conflicting scenarios and loses its value as a decision tool.

The governance problem with scenarios

o9 makes it easy to create scenarios. That is a feature — but it creates a governance challenge. Without rules around scenario creation and lifecycle, the planning environment can accumulate:

  • Dozens of scenarios with no clear owner or status
  • Scenarios built on outdated assumptions that are still visible to the organisation
  • Conflicting scenarios that present different views of the future without any indication of which is most credible
  • Scenarios promoted to baseline without appropriate sign-off

Scenario governance is the set of rules that prevents this degradation.

What scenario governance covers

Creation rules — who is authorised to create scenarios, and for what purpose. Typically, planners can create working scenarios for their own analysis; scenarios intended for executive review require naming conventions and metadata standards that allow them to be found and understood by others.

Naming and documentation standards — a scenario named “scenario_v3_final_FINAL” is not useful to anyone. Governance should specify: a descriptive name that explains the assumption being tested, the date created, the owner, and a brief description of the key inputs changed.

Review and sign-off process — scenarios presented in S&OP or S&OE meetings should be reviewed and approved before they enter those forums. An unreviewed scenario can introduce incorrect conclusions into executive decision-making.

Promotion rules — promoting a scenario to the baseline plan is the most consequential action in the planning cycle. Governance should specify who has the authority to promote (typically planning leadership after S&OP sign-off), what validation is required before promotion, and how the old baseline is archived.

Archive and deletion — scenarios that are no longer relevant should be archived or deleted. A clean scenario environment is a usable one.

The role of the planning owner

Someone needs to own the scenario environment. In most organisations this is the planning manager or a lead planner. Their responsibilities include: enforcing naming and documentation standards, reviewing the scenario list before S&OP, ensuring that only current and relevant scenarios are visible, and managing the promotion process.

Scenario governance as maturity indicator

The state of the scenario environment is a reliable indicator of planning maturity. An organisation with a clean, well-documented set of current scenarios and a clear promotion history is operating a mature planning process. One with dozens of unnamed scenarios from six months ago is not — regardless of what the system is technically capable of.
