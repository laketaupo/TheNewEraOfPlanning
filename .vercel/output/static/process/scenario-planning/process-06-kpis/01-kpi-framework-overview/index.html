KPI Framework Overview

A KPI framework for supply chain planning covers four domains: service, inventory, efficiency, and forecast accuracy. Understanding the structure prevents the common trap of measuring everything while managing nothing.

What a KPI framework is

A KPI framework is the organised set of metrics the organisation uses to measure planning performance. The word “framework” matters: it implies structure and hierarchy, not just a list. A good KPI framework has a small number of primary KPIs that reflect overall performance, supported by a larger set of diagnostic metrics that explain why the primary KPIs move.

The failure mode of most KPI implementations is measuring too many things with equal weight. When everything is a KPI, nothing is managed. The framework should force a choice: what are the three to five metrics that, if the planning team improves them, the business unambiguously gets better?

The four planning KPI domains

Service — measuring whether the organisation delivers to customers as promised.

  • On-Time In-Full (OTIF) — the percentage of orders delivered on time and in full
  • Fill rate — the percentage of demand fulfilled from available stock
  • Order line fill rate — fill rate measured at the individual order line level

Inventory — measuring how efficiently the organisation uses working capital.

  • Inventory turns — how many times per year the stock is replenished and sold
  • Days on Hand (DoH) — the number of days of forward demand covered by current stock
  • Excess and obsolete inventory (E&O) — the value of stock that is past its use date or unlikely to be sold

Forecast accuracy — measuring how well the organisation predicts demand.

  • Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE) — the average magnitude of forecast error as a percentage of actual demand
  • Forecast bias — whether the forecast is systematically too high or too low
  • Forecast accuracy at different horizons — a 12-week forecast is typically less accurate than a 4-week forecast; measuring both reveals where accuracy degrades

Planning efficiency — measuring the effectiveness of the planning process itself.

  • Plan adherence — the percentage of the published plan that is actually executed without deviation
  • Planner workload (exceptions per planner) — an indicator of whether the team is managing by exception or drowning in noise
  • Cycle time — how long it takes to complete the monthly S&OP cycle

Leading vs. lagging indicators

Service and inventory KPIs are largely lagging: they measure what has already happened. Forecast accuracy and plan adherence are more leading: they predict where service and inventory problems are heading. A planning team that only reviews lagging KPIs is looking in the rearview mirror. One that integrates leading indicators can intervene before a service failure materialises.
