Operating Model

How S&OP and S&OE work — the governance layer, meeting cadence, and how plans move from decision to execution.

  1. What Is S&OP?

    Sales & Operations Planning is the monthly process that aligns demand, supply, and finance into a single agreed plan — the authoritative version of what the business intends to do.

    Process · Operating Model 5 min

  2. What Is S&OE?

    Sales & Operations Execution is the weekly process that manages the near-term horizon — resolving exceptions, adjusting short-term supply, and keeping execution aligned with the S&OP plan.

    Process · Operating Model 4 min

  3. Execution: Translating Plans into Action

    Execution is where the plan becomes reality — purchase orders, production runs, shipments. Understanding how plans flow into execution systems prevents the gap between what was planned and what was done.

    Process · Operating Model 4 min

  4. The Planning Cadence

    A well-designed meeting rhythm connects monthly S&OP, weekly S&OE, and daily execution. The cadence is the operating model in practice — the recurring structure that keeps the organisation aligned.

    Process · Operating Model 4 min

Process Design

How the planning processes are structured — decision frameworks, escalation paths, exception handling, and service level design.

  1. The Scenario Planning Process

    A good scenario planning process has four steps: define the question, model the assumption, compare the outputs, and decide. Skipping steps creates confusion.

    Process · Scenario Planning Fundamentals 4 min

  2. Service Level Targets

    Service level targets define how reliably the organisation commits to fulfilling customer demand. They are a policy decision that balances customer satisfaction against inventory cost.

    Process · Planning Policy 4 min

  3. Decision Framework

    A decision framework defines which planning decisions are made by whom, at what level, and within what authority. It prevents confusion, reduces escalation bottlenecks, and ensures accountability is clear.

    Process · Governance & Escalation 4 min

  4. Escalation Paths

    Escalation paths define how planning issues move from the team that identifies them to the level of authority needed to resolve them. Clear paths prevent issues from stalling and ensure decisions happen in time to matter.

    Process · Governance & Escalation 4 min

  5. Management by Exception

    Exception-based working is the practice of directing planner attention to deviations from plan rather than reviewing everything. It makes a large planning model manageable and focuses effort where it matters most.

    Process · Governance & Escalation 5 min

Data Requirements

What data the plan depends on — the data model, where data comes from, how it flows in, and what happens when it's wrong.

  1. What is Data-Driven Planning?

    A planning process is only as good as the data it runs on. This topic defines what data-driven planning means in practice.

    Data · Planning Data Fundamentals 4 min

  2. Where Data Comes From

    Planning data doesn't originate in Planning software — it is loaded from source systems. Understanding the sources helps you understand why data problems occur.

    Data · Planning Data Fundamentals 3 min

  3. How Data Flows into a Plan

    From raw data load to a calculated plan — the sequence of how Planning software processes incoming data and translates it into planning outputs.

    Data · Planning Data Fundamentals 4 min

  4. The Cost of Bad Data

    Bad planning data doesn't stay in the system — it becomes inventory, stockouts, and wasted capacity. Understanding the downstream effects makes data quality feel urgent.

    Data · Planning Data Fundamentals 4 min

  5. Master Data

    Master data is the stable, reference information that describes the objects in the planning model — items, locations, customers, suppliers. It changes slowly but when it's wrong, everything built on it is wrong.

    Data · Data Types in Planning 4 min

  6. Transactional Data

    Transactional data records what has happened: inventory movements, order receipts, production completions. It is the evidence of execution — and the foundation for keeping the plan grounded in reality.

    Data · Data Types in Planning 4 min

  7. Planning Parameters

    Planning parameters are the settings that control how the planning engine behaves for each item: lead times, safety stock, minimum order quantities, lot sizes. Getting them right is as important as having good data.

    Data · Data Types in Planning 5 min

Data Governance

How data quality is owned, measured, and improved — from defining good enough to assigning accountability and building confidence.

  1. What Makes Data "Good Enough"?

    Data quality isn't binary. Four dimensions — completeness, accuracy, timeliness, and consistency — define whether data is fit for planning.

    Data · Data Quality & Its Impact 4 min

  2. Common Data Problems in Planning

    The same data problems appear in almost every supply chain planning implementation. Recognising them is the first step to fixing them.

    Data · Data Quality & Its Impact 5 min

  3. Data Governance Basics

    Data quality doesn't maintain itself. Governance defines who is responsible for which data, how changes are approved, and how issues are escalated.

    Data · Data Quality & Its Impact 4 min

  4. Data Confidence

    Data confidence is the degree of trust that planners place in the data they are working with. Low-confidence data undermines decisions; understanding where confidence is low is the first step to improving it.

    Data · Data Governance 4 min

  5. Data Ownership

    Every data element needs an owner — someone accountable for its accuracy, completeness, and currency. Without clear ownership, data quality degrades and no one is responsible for fixing it.

    Data · Data Governance 3 min

  6. Data Definitions & Single Source of Truth

    When the same concept means different things in different systems or to different people, planning decisions get made on inconsistent data. Clear definitions and a single source of truth are the foundation of data reliability.

    Data · Data Governance 4 min

System Design

How the technology is architected — the data model, system interfaces, batch processing, and how ERP and planning software integrate.

  1. The Planning software Data Model Overview

    Planning software represents the supply chain as a graph of connected nodes. Understanding this structure is the foundation for understanding how data works.

    Data · Planning Data Fundamentals 4 min

  2. System Interfaces Overview

    The four systems in the landscape — ERP, Planning software, FMS, and MDM system — are connected by a set of defined interfaces. Understanding what flows where is the foundation for troubleshooting and governance.

    Technology · End-to-End Architecture 5 min

  3. Batch Runs

    Batch runs are the scheduled jobs that keep the planning model current — refreshing transactional data, triggering planning engine recalculations, and synchronising master data.

    Technology · End-to-End Architecture 4 min

  4. Data Upload

    Not all data flows through automated interfaces. Manual data uploads handle one-off corrections, new data sources, and situations where the automated interface is not yet available.

    Technology · End-to-End Architecture 3 min

  5. ERP ↔ Planning software Integration

    ERP and Planning software exchange transactional data in both directions. ERP sends actuals; Planning software sends planned orders. Understanding the flow prevents discrepancies between what is planned and what is being executed.

    Technology · System Integration 4 min

  6. Planning software ↔ FMS & MDM system

    FMS feeds field-level supply signals into Planning software; MDM system feeds the master data that makes those signals interpretable. Understanding these integrations is essential for data quality management.

    Technology · System Integration 4 min

Advanced Planning

Scenario analysis, what-if simulation, and the policy decisions — safety stock, allocation, prioritisation — that sharpen the supply plan.

  1. What is Scenario Planning?

    Scenario planning is the discipline of exploring alternative futures before committing to a course of action. In Planning software, it means running parallel plans.

    Process · Scenario Planning Fundamentals 4 min

  2. Setting Up Effective Scenarios

    The quality of a scenario depends on how it is framed. Good scenarios are specific, comparable, and bounded.

    Process · Scenario Planning Fundamentals 3 min

  3. Scenarios for Supply Risks

    Supply-side disruptions — supplier failures, capacity shortfalls, logistics delays — require specific scenario types that model constrained supply and test your planning resilience.

    Process · Scenario Planning Fundamentals 4 min

  4. Creating a Scenario in Planning software

    A scenario in Planning software starts as a copy of the baseline plan. This topic covers how to create one, name it, and scope it correctly before making any changes.

    Process · Running Scenarios in Planning Software 4 min

  5. Adjusting Parameters & Assumptions

    The substance of a scenario is the change you make to the model. This topic covers the main levers — demand, supply, capacity, and time — and how to adjust them precisely.

    Process · Running Scenarios in Planning Software 5 min

  6. Running & Comparing Scenarios

    After making adjustments, you run the planning engine inside the scenario and compare the results against the baseline. This is where the value of scenario planning becomes visible.

    Process · Running Scenarios in Planning Software 5 min

  7. Safety Stock Policy

    Safety stock is the buffer inventory held to absorb demand and supply variability. The policy determines how much to hold, where to hold it, and when to replenish it.

    Process · Planning Policy 5 min

  8. Allocation Policy

    When supply cannot meet all demand, allocation policy determines which customers get which quantities. Defining this in advance prevents ad hoc decisions under pressure and ensures commercial priorities are respected.

    Process · Planning Policy 4 min

  9. Prioritization Frameworks

    Prioritization determines which orders, products, and customers are served first when resources are constrained. A clear framework prevents conflict and ensures the planning system reflects actual business strategy.

    Process · Planning Policy 4 min

Continuous Improvement

How to sustain and improve performance — KPI frameworks, exception management, data quality cycles, and scenario governance.

  1. KPI Framework Overview

    A KPI framework for supply chain planning covers four domains: service, inventory, efficiency, and forecast accuracy. Understanding the structure prevents the common trap of measuring everything while managing nothing.

    Process · KPIs 4 min

  2. Key Planning KPIs Defined

    Precise definitions for the most important supply chain planning KPIs — what each metric measures, how it is calculated, and what a good result looks like.

    Process · KPIs 5 min

  3. KPI Ownership and Review Cadence

    KPIs only drive behaviour if someone owns them and they are reviewed regularly. Assigning ownership and embedding KPI review into the planning cadence transforms metrics from reports into management tools.

    Process · KPIs 3 min

  4. KPIs and Exception Management

    KPIs and exception-based working are two sides of the same management system. KPIs measure outcomes; exceptions drive the daily actions that determine those outcomes.

    Process · KPIs 3 min

  5. Improving Data Quality Over Time

    Data quality is not a one-time clean-up — it is an ongoing capability. This topic introduces the practices that sustain planning data quality as the business evolves.

    Data · Data Quality & Its Impact 4 min
