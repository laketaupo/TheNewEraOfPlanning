Building an Effective Planning Team

What separates high-performing planning teams from the rest — and the practical steps to get there.

Right-size the team to the business

There is no universal headcount for a planning team. A single-site business with a few hundred SKUs might run with one demand planner, one supply planner, and a part-time S&OP lead. A global business with thousands of SKUs across dozens of plants needs dedicated managers, multiple planners per region, and analysts to keep the data clean. The mistake is copying another company’s structure without matching it to your own complexity — either drowning a small business in roles it doesn’t need, or stretching three people across a problem that needs ten.

Clear ownership beats shared ownership

High-performing teams have sharp lines of accountability. Each role owns a specific output — the forecast, the supply plan, the financial reconciliation — and everyone knows who that is. Struggling teams tend to have the opposite: everyone touches everything, nobody owns anything, and when a number is wrong there’s no single person responsible for fixing it. “The team owns the plan” sounds collaborative, but in practice it means no one does.

Common failure modes

A few patterns show up again and again in teams that underperform:

  • Planners spending 80% of their time gathering and cleaning data instead of analysing it and making decisions. If your skilled planners are effectively manual data integrators, the value of the role is wasted.
  • No dedicated S&OP lead. When the cycle is run “on the side” by someone with another full-time job, the cross-functional alignment quietly degrades and reviews become status updates instead of decisions.
  • Demand and supply working in silos. When the two sides only meet in the formal review, problems surface too late to act on.
  • No analyst layer, leaving managers and planners to maintain master data and reporting themselves.

Practical steps to build a strong team

  1. Map roles to outcomes, not job titles. Start from the outputs the plan needs and assign clear ownership for each one before worrying about org structure.
  2. Free planners to plan. Invest in data quality and automation so that skilled people spend their time on judgement, not janitorial data work.
  3. Appoint a real S&OP lead. Give the cycle a dedicated owner with the authority to drive consensus and escalate conflict.
  4. Build the cross-functional muscle. Bring finance and commercial in early and often, so the plan is a shared agreement rather than a supply chain document everyone else ignores.

Get these right and the team becomes more than the sum of its roles — a group that turns data into decisions the whole business trusts.
