The Planning Team · Topic 6

The Broader Organisation Structure

The planning team sits within a larger organisational context. Understanding how the company is structured — and where planning fits — clarifies decision-making authority and stakeholder relationships.

Why organisational structure matters for planners

Planning does not happen in isolation. Every planning decision touches other functions: a supply constraint affects what sales can commit to; a demand forecast depends on inputs from commercial; an allocation decision requires sign-off from leadership. Knowing who sits where in the organisation — and what authority they hold — determines how planning decisions get made and escalated.

A planner who understands the organisational structure makes better decisions faster: they know who to call when a supply issue needs commercial input, who needs to approve an exception, and whose sign-off is required before a scenario is promoted to the baseline.

The functional structure relevant to planning

Planning typically sits within the Supply Chain or Operations function, but interacts heavily with four others:

Commercial / Sales — owns the demand signal. The commercial team manages customer relationships, holds the sales forecast, and makes commitments to customers. In S&OP, the commercial team presents the demand review. Alignment between planning and commercial on the demand signal is foundational to a credible plan.

Operations / Manufacturing — owns supply execution. The operations team runs the production processes, manages the production schedule, and reports on actual output. Planning creates the proposed production schedule; operations executes it and feeds back deviations.

Finance — owns the financial plan. The financial plan (budget, reforecast) needs to be aligned with the operational plan produced in S&OP. Finance participates in the executive S&OP to ensure that the agreed plan is financially sound and consistent with approved budgets.

Procurement — owns supplier relationships. Procurement negotiates contracts, manages supplier performance, and resolves supply issues. Planning signals what needs to be ordered; procurement determines whether the supply base can deliver it.

The planning team in the structure

The planning team’s reporting line varies by organisation. It may sit under:

  • Supply Chain Director / Head of Supply Chain
  • Operations Director
  • Chief Operating Officer (COO)

Regardless of reporting line, the planning team acts as a cross-functional integrator: it receives inputs from commercial (demand), operations (supply), and finance (constraints) and produces an integrated plan that all functions can execute against.

Navigating the structure in practice

Understanding the structure means knowing when to escalate and to whom. A shortage that requires a commercial decision (which customer loses supply) escalates to the commercial leadership. A capacity decision that requires capital expenditure escalates to the COO or CFO. A demand signal that conflicts with the financial plan escalates to the joint commercial-finance forum.

The decision framework (covered in the Governance chapter) should map these escalation routes explicitly so that planners do not need to rediscover the structure each time an issue arises.
