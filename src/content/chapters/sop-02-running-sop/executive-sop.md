## The executive review's sole purpose

The executive S&OP review exists to make decisions that operational teams cannot make themselves. It is not a status update. It is not a performance review. It is not an escalation forum for last month's problems. Every minute spent on items that should have been resolved in pre-S&OP is a minute not spent on the strategic trade-offs that only the leadership team can resolve.

## What a good executive review looks like

- **Pre-read circulated 48 hours in advance** — no surprises in the room; participants arrive informed
- **Runs to time** — typically 60–90 minutes; a well-prepared review rarely needs more
- **Structured around decisions** — the pack presents options and recommendations, not narrative summaries
- **Ends with commitments** — each decision is recorded with an owner and a date; the record is binding

## The facilitator's role

The S&OP process owner or IBP manager facilitates the executive review. Their job is to keep the discussion on the decision items, prevent relitigating resolved issues, and ensure every item ends with a clear decision or a documented deferral with a reason and a timeline.

## Financial integration

In a mature S&OP, the CFO or finance lead presents a **financial bridge** at the executive review: here is the operational plan, here is what it implies for revenue, margin, and working capital, and here is the gap to budget. This turns the executive review into a genuine business planning forum, not just a supply chain meeting.

## Common failure modes

**Status presentations instead of decisions** — packs that report what happened last month rather than presenting the options for next month. The executive cannot act on history; they need options and recommendations.

**The CEO's favourite KPI** — executive reviews that spend 45 minutes on one metric because the CEO is interested in it, leaving no time for the actual decision items. The facilitator must protect the agenda.

**Decisions without commitment** — "we'll look into it" is not a decision. Every item leaving the executive review must have a clear yes/no/deferred outcome, with an owner if deferred.

**Overriding pre-S&OP resolutions** — if the executive routinely reverses decisions made at a lower level without strong justification, it undermines the authority of the pre-S&OP process and creates a culture of escalation for everything.
