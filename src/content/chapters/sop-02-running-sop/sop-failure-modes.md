## The process exists but doesn't work

Many organisations have an S&OP calendar, an S&OP pack, and an S&OP meeting. But the decisions made in those meetings don't stick, the numbers aren't trusted, and the process feels like overhead. The meetings happen; alignment doesn't.

The causes are almost always the same.

## Data is not trusted

If participants spend the demand review arguing about which spreadsheet has the right numbers, no meaningful discussion is possible. Data credibility is foundational. Planning software helps — a single live model means everyone is looking at the same numbers — but only if the model is maintained with discipline and the data inputs are clean.

## The wrong people attend

A demand review without commercial ownership is a statistics exercise. An executive S&OP without the CFO is a supply chain update. When key decision-makers send delegates who lack authority to commit the business, decisions get deferred, relitigated, or quietly reversed.

## S&OP and budget are disconnected

If the S&OP plan and the financial budget are separate documents managed by separate teams, the executive review has no financial grounding. Supply chain can optimise volume while finance manages a completely different number. The financial bridge — the reconciliation between operational volumes and financial outcomes — is what turns S&OP into a business management process rather than a supply chain exercise.

## The process gets pulled into execution

The most common failure mode: the monthly S&OP reviews get hijacked by last week's operational crisis. Leadership time that should go to 6–18 month trade-offs gets consumed by yesterday's shipment miss. This happens when there is no S&OE process to absorb and contain weekly operational issues — or when the S&OE process exists but leadership doesn't trust it to handle issues without their involvement.

## No cadence discipline

When the S&OP calendar is flexible — when the demand review moves because a key person is travelling, when the executive review is shortened to 20 minutes, when months are skipped — the process loses credibility. The supply chain cannot plan if the planning cycle is unpredictable.

## The plan is not published

S&OP decisions that are made but not loaded into operating systems and communicated to execution teams have no effect on the business. The cycle is only complete when the agreed plan updates inventory targets, procurement positions, and capacity commitments in the systems that people actually work in.

## What comes next

S&OP sets the plan — but the plan starts drifting from reality the moment it is published. The S&OE process is the weekly discipline that keeps supply and demand aligned between monthly S&OP cycles. It manages the exceptions, absorbs the disruptions, and escalates back to S&OP only when a deviation is too large to resolve tactically.

If S&OE is working well, it is the reason your S&OP meetings stay focused on the long-term plan rather than last week's fires. The next module explores how it works.
