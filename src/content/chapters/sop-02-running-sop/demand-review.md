## The purpose of the demand review

The demand review is not a forecasting meeting. The statistical forecast has already been run. The demand review is where the commercial team validates, challenges, and enriches the statistical baseline with information the model cannot see: upcoming promotions, customer conversations, distribution wins, competitive moves.

The output is a **single consensus number** — not a range, not multiple versions, not "it depends." The business needs one demand plan to plan supply against.

## Agenda structure

A well-run demand review covers:

1. **Actuals vs. forecast** — how did last month perform? Where was the model wrong, and why?
2. **Baseline forecast review** — what is the statistical model projecting, and does it look reasonable?
3. **Commercial overlays** — what adjustments is the commercial team making, and what assumptions underpin them?
4. **Exception resolution** — items flagged as requiring discussion (large volume changes, new launches, lost accounts)
5. **Consensus sign-off** — the demand plan is agreed and locked for supply review

## What good looks like

A demand review that is working well:
- Runs on time (typically 60–90 minutes)
- Has a pre-reviewed pack so participants arrive informed
- Surfaces disagreements and resolves them in the meeting
- Produces a forecast with documented assumptions — not just numbers
- Ends with ownership of any outstanding actions

## Common failure modes

**Over-reliance on the model** — treating the statistical forecast as the answer rather than a starting point. The model has no visibility of a sales team's pipeline or a key customer's buying change.

**Sandbagging** — sales teams systematically underestimating demand to protect themselves from missing targets. This leads to chronic under-supply and erodes trust in the S&OP process.

**Too much detail** — spending 90 minutes reviewing individual SKUs instead of exception management at product family level. The demand review operates at aggregate; detailed SKU issues belong in weekly planning.

## Locking the demand plan

At the end of the demand review, the demand plan is locked. This does not mean it cannot be revised — it means any revision requires a documented change request with an owner and a reason. This discipline prevents the plan from being quietly adjusted by individual functions after the review.
