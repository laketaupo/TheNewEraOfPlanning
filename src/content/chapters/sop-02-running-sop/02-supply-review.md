## What the supply review does

The supply review answers one question: can we fulfil the consensus demand plan, and at what cost? Operations — manufacturing, procurement, logistics — reviews available capacity, material constraints, and current inventory against the agreed demand plan and identifies where the plan is feasible, where it is constrained, and what it would cost to close the gaps.

The supply review does not make decisions about trade-offs. Its job is to prepare the facts and options so that the pre-S&OP and executive review can decide.

## Key inputs

- The locked consensus demand plan from the demand review
- Current inventory positions and projected coverage
- Manufacturing capacity: current utilisation, bottlenecks, available upside
- Supplier lead times and committed material positions
- Logistics constraints: warehouse capacity, carrier availability, export restrictions

In planning software, these inputs are drawn from the live planning model — the system runs a constrained plan automatically and produces a gap report before the review begins.

## What the supply review produces

- A **feasibility assessment** by product family: can supply meet demand in the planning horizon?
- A **gap report**: where shortfalls are projected, by volume, value, and timing
- A **costed options list**: for each significant gap, what options exist and what do they cost? (e.g., overtime, expedited procurement, external sourcing, demand shaping)
- Items requiring executive decision — gaps that cannot be closed within operations authority

## Agenda structure

1. **Current inventory and coverage review** — where do we stand today?
2. **Capacity utilisation** — is manufacturing running at, over, or under capacity?
3. **Constrained supply plan review** — where does supply fall short against the consensus demand plan?
4. **Options analysis** — what can operations do to close the gaps, and at what cost?
5. **Escalation list** — what items need to go to pre-S&OP for cross-functional resolution?

## Common failure modes

**Presenting gaps without options** — telling the business there is a supply shortfall with no proposed solutions is not a supply review; it is a problem statement. The supply team should always arrive with costed options, even if none of them are ideal.

**Hiding capacity problems** — optimism bias in capacity assessment leads to agreed plans that operations knows it cannot deliver. If a constraint is real, it must be on the table.

**Re-running the demand review** — the supply review should accept the consensus demand plan as given. Relitigating demand numbers in the supply review adds no value and wastes time.
