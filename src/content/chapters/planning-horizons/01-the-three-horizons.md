## Why horizons matter

Not all planning decisions are the same. Some decisions — like which markets to serve or how much capacity to build — take months to implement and years to pay off. Others — like which orders to pick today — need to be made and acted on within hours. Trying to manage all of these with a single process or cadence creates noise at best and paralysis at worst.

Planning horizons exist to match the right level of decision-making to the right time frame. Each horizon has different data, different certainty, and different owners. Understanding which horizon you are working in is one of the most practical skills a planner can develop.

## Long-term: strategic horizon (12–36 months)

The long-term horizon covers roughly 12 to 36 months out, though this varies by industry. At this horizon, the focus is on structural decisions: capacity investments, network design, new product introductions, market expansion, and supplier contracts.

Decisions here are low-frequency but high-impact. The forecast is uncertain — you are working with assumptions and scenarios, not a precise number. That uncertainty is acceptable because the decisions being made are about direction, not precision.

Ownership at this horizon sits with senior leadership and, in some organisations, a dedicated strategic planning function. The finance plan and the sales plan converge here into a long-range business plan.

## Mid-term: tactical horizon (3–12 months)

The tactical horizon is where S&OP lives. Over a 3 to 12-month window, the plan becomes specific enough to drive real commitments: production schedules, replenishment orders, workforce adjustments, and budget allocation.

This is still an aggregate-level plan — by product family and month, not by SKU and day — but it is specific enough to test against actual capacity and supply constraints. The S&OP cycle updates this plan monthly, balancing demand and supply and surfacing gaps before they become crises.

Ownership at this horizon is cross-functional: demand planning, supply planning, finance, and commercial teams all contribute. The output is a single agreed plan that the whole organisation works from.

## Short-term: operational horizon (0–12 weeks)

The operational horizon covers the immediate future — typically zero to twelve weeks. At this level, the plan meets reality. Customer orders are confirmed, production runs are scheduled, inventory is positioned, and exceptions are managed in near real time.

This is the S&OE and execution domain. Decisions are high-frequency and detail-level. Uncertainty about what needs to happen has collapsed — what remains uncertain is whether the plan will hold given real-world disruptions.

Ownership here sits with operational planners, schedulers, and logistics teams. The focus shifts from planning to monitoring and responding.

## How the horizons nest

The three horizons are not separate plans — they are the same plan at different levels of resolution. The long-term horizon sets the strategic boundaries. The mid-term plan fills in the detail within those boundaries. The short-term plan executes within the mid-term commitments.

When this nesting works well, decisions made in S&OP are specific enough to guide S&OE, and execution teams are not making up constraints that should have been decided earlier. When it breaks down, each horizon becomes its own island — and the organisation reacts rather than plans.
