## What the review produces

The integrated S&OE review is a decision-making meeting, not a reporting meeting. Its value is measured not by the quality of the presentations but by the quality of the decisions made and the speed at which they are implemented. Three outputs capture the decisions and make them actionable: an updated near-term plan, a decision log, and an escalation list.

## Updated near-term plan

The primary output of the integrated S&OE review is a near-term plan that reflects the decisions made in the meeting. Where decisions have been made to adjust order priorities, redirect stock, expedite supply, or modify the production schedule, those adjustments are made in the planning system during or immediately after the meeting — not deferred to a later update cycle.

An updated plan that takes 48 hours to be reflected in the system is not actually updated. Execution teams — warehouse operations, production scheduling, customer service — make decisions based on the plan as they see it. If the review decisions do not appear in the system promptly, those decisions do not reach the people who need to act on them.

The discipline of updating the plan immediately after the review is one of the distinguishing marks of a mature S&OE process. It requires that the S&OE planner has system access and the authority to make near-term plan changes without further approval — and that this authority is understood and respected by adjacent functions.

## Decision log

Every decision made in the integrated S&OE review should be recorded in a brief decision log: the item decided, the decision taken, the owner of the resulting action, and the expected completion date. The decision log does not need to be elaborate — a structured note in the planning system or a shared document is sufficient — but it must be written during the meeting, not reconstructed from memory afterward.

The decision log serves three purposes. It is an accountability record: the next week's review opens by checking the status of each logged action. It is a communication tool: team members who were not in the meeting can see what was decided and what is expected of them. And it is an audit trail: when a decision turns out to be wrong, the log shows what information was available at the time and what logic was applied, which is essential for learning and process improvement.

## Escalation list with owner and timeline

Escalation items are a distinct output from the decision log. A decision item is resolved within S&OE. An escalation item is transferred to S&OP because it cannot be resolved within the near-term planning horizon or within the authority of the S&OE team.

Each escalation item is recorded with: a description of the issue, the evidence that makes it an S&OE-level situation rather than an S&OE-resolvable one, the owner of the escalation within the S&OE team (who will carry it forward), and the timeline by which a response from S&OP is needed. Vague escalations — "we have a supply issue" without specifics — are not useful. A useful escalation gives the receiving S&OP team everything they need to add the item to the right part of their monthly review agenda.

## How outputs flow to execution teams

The updated near-term plan in the planning system is the primary channel through which execution teams receive S&OE decisions. Production scheduling, warehouse management, and customer service teams work from the plan; changes to the plan are their instruction to act differently.

For decisions that require action outside the planning system — an email to a supplier, a call to a customer, a conversation with the production floor — the decision log serves as the prompt. The named action owner follows up on their assigned item before the next review cycle.

The S&OE planner is responsible for confirming that action owners have received and understood their assignments, particularly for time-sensitive items. A logged action with a named owner is not a guarantee of follow-through; the planner's role includes tracking completion during the week.

## Linking S&OE to the S&OP cycle

The integrated S&OE review is not an island. It sits within a planning hierarchy that runs from daily execution through S&OE to S&OP. The review's escalation output is the formal link between S&OE and S&OP.

Escalation items accumulated over the month become an input to the S&OP demand review and supply review. Patterns in the escalation list — the same supplier recurring week after week, a particular product family consistently running above plan, a location repeatedly short of stock — are evidence of structural planning problems that the monthly S&OP cycle should address.

In this way, the integrated S&OE review does more than manage the current week. Over time, it surfaces the structural gaps in the plan that S&OP needs to fix, creating a feedback loop that makes both processes progressively more effective.
