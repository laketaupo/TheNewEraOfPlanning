## Where integration lives or dies

A planning hierarchy with well-designed levels can still fail if the handoffs between those levels are poor. A handoff is the point where one level's output becomes the next level's input. It requires a clear protocol: what information is transferred, in what format, at what cadence, and who owns any gaps between what was planned and what was received.

There are three critical handoffs in the integrated planning model.

## Handoff 1: S&OP to S&OE

At the end of each S&OP cycle, the agreed monthly plan — covering volume by product family, supply targets, inventory positions, and financial commitments — becomes the working plan for S&OE.

S&OE receives this plan and translates it into weekly operational targets. It breaks aggregate volume into SKU-level weekly buckets, confirms the targets against current inventory and supplier positions, and identifies any gaps between what S&OP assumed and what is actually available.

This handoff fails when S&OP produces a plan at too high a level of aggregation to be actionable, when the plan is not made available to S&OE in a timely way, or when S&OE routinely overrides the S&OP plan without escalation. When it breaks down, S&OE becomes an improvising function rather than an executing one — and the S&OP process loses credibility.

## Handoff 2: S&OE to execution

Each week, S&OE produces a firm near-term plan: production priorities, replenishment orders, delivery commitments. This plan is handed to execution — the shop floor, the warehouse, the procurement team — as the authoritative set of priorities for the coming days.

For execution to work with this plan, it needs to be both specific enough to act on and stable enough to trust. A plan that changes every day provides neither. The time fence structure exists precisely to protect this handoff: the firm fence ensures that what execution receives on Monday has not been revised by Tuesday.

This handoff fails when S&OE does not produce a clear priority list, when the plan changes inside the firm fence without authorisation, or when execution teams have no channel to report deviations back to S&OE. The result is that execution teams stop trusting the plan and start making their own decisions — which are invisible to the planning layers above them.

## Handoff 3: Execution back to planning

The least visible handoff is the one that flows upward: actuals from execution back into the planning model. This includes production output, inventory movements, confirmed shipments, supplier delivery performance, and any exceptions that were resolved at execution level.

Without this feedback, the planning model drifts. S&OP and S&OE are planning against assumptions that no longer reflect reality. Safety stock parameters are set against variability data that is months stale. Forecast accuracy cannot improve because there is no clean signal of what actually happened.

This handoff fails when actual data is not captured systematically, when it is not fed back into the planning system in a timely way, or when exceptions resolved at execution level are never reviewed to understand whether they indicate a systemic issue that S&OE or S&OP should address.

## What poor handoffs cost

The cost of poor handoffs compounds across the hierarchy. When S&OP-to-S&OE is broken, S&OE must improvise — and the S&OP plan becomes a document rather than a working commitment. When S&OE-to-execution is broken, execution operates from memory and habit rather than current priorities. When execution does not feed actuals back, the entire planning model degrades over time.

Diagnosing planning dysfunction almost always leads back to one or more of these three handoffs. Fixing the process structures around each one — the meeting cadence, the data transfers, the escalation protocols — is the practical work of building an integrated planning capability.
