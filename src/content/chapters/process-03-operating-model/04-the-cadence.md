## What a cadence is

A planning cadence is the recurring schedule of meetings, reviews, and decisions that keep the planning cycle running. It is not just a calendar — it is the operational backbone of the planning operating model. A strong cadence ensures that the right decisions are made at the right frequency by the right people.

Without a cadence, planning degrades into ad hoc firefighting: whoever shouts loudest gets attention, long-term issues never get reviewed, and plans drift from reality without anyone noticing.

## The three-level cadence

A standard planning cadence has three levels that nest together:

**Monthly — S&OP cycle**
- Demand review (Week 1 of the month)
- Supply review (Week 2)
- Pre-S&OP reconciliation (Week 3)
- Executive S&OP sign-off (Week 4)
- Plan publication and ERP update (end of month)

**Weekly — S&OE cycle**
- Exception review: items at risk in the 1–6 week horizon
- Corrective action assignment and tracking
- Short-horizon capacity and supply resolution
- Update to o9 planning model where needed

**Daily — Execution layer**
- Production order status review
- Inbound shipment tracking
- Customer order fulfilment monitoring
- Exception escalation to S&OE where the issue cannot be resolved within the day

## Designing the cadence for your organisation

The cadence above is a template, not a prescription. In practice, the right cadence depends on the length of your planning horizon, the variability of your demand and supply, and the size and structure of your planning team.

For agricultural businesses with long growing cycles, the monthly S&OP cycle often needs to be supplemented with **seasonal planning reviews** — deep dives that happen quarterly or at key crop cycle milestones rather than on a calendar month basis.

## The cadence in o9

o9 supports the cadence through its workflow and collaboration features: scenarios can be locked and published after S&OP sign-off, exceptions can be assigned to owners for S&OE resolution, and plan versions can be tracked across the monthly cycle. The system does not enforce the cadence — it is the people and process that make it work — but it provides the structure within which the cadence operates.
