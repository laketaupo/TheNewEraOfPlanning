## The nature of demand uncertainty

Forecasts are always wrong. The question is not whether demand will deviate from the plan, but by how much, in which direction, and for which products. Demand uncertainty scenarios exist to answer: "if the forecast is wrong, how bad does it get, and what would we do about it?"

For agricultural supply chains — with long growing cycles, weather dependency, and concentrated customers — demand uncertainty compounds. An early planting season, a retailer tender win, or a new crop variety adoption can shift volumes significantly within a planning horizon.

## Types of demand uncertainty scenarios

**Upside demand scenario** models what happens if one or more customers order more than the baseline forecast. The key outputs are: where capacity or stock becomes the binding constraint, which product lines are affected first, and whether the supply plan can flex to absorb the upside.

**Downside demand scenario** models a demand shortfall — a customer delays their programme, a crop season runs shorter than expected, or a variety fails to achieve adoption. The outputs are: where excess inventory will accumulate, what the financial exposure is, and what demand reallocation options exist.

**Customer concentration risk** models the loss or reduction of a single large customer. For businesses where a handful of customers represent a large share of volume, this scenario tests resilience without needing a broad demand collapse assumption.

**Product mix shift** models demand moving between product variants — more of one crop variety and less of another, or a shift toward smaller pack sizes. Even when total volume is unchanged, mix shifts can stress capacity at specific process steps or create surplus in one SKU while constraining another.

## Connecting demand scenarios to supply decisions

The output of a demand scenario is not a new forecast — it is a decision trigger. A well-structured demand uncertainty scenario should answer:

1. **Do we have enough supply capacity to meet the upside?** If not, when should we commit to building that capacity?
2. **What is the inventory risk on the downside?** If demand falls, which stocks are stranded and for how long?
3. **What are the decision points?** At what point in the timeline does the decision become irreversible — a production run committed, a seed treatment started?

In Planning software, these questions are answered by comparing scenario outputs side-by-side against the baseline plan, giving planners a quantified view of the exposure in each direction before committing resources.
