## System Interactions at a Glance

| System | What they read | What they enter | What they are responsible for |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Planning Software** | Supply plan, exception list, planned orders, inventory projections, capacity utilisation | Adjustments to planned orders, simulation inputs, supply confirmations, constraint overrides | Maintaining an executable supply plan; acting on exceptions within agreed horizons |
| **ERP** | Open purchase orders, production order status, goods receipt confirmations, current inventory positions | Released orders (pushed from Planning Software) | Reconciling ERP actuals against the Planning Software view; ensuring released orders are accurate |
| **MDM system** | BOMs, lead times, safety stock parameters, item master attributes | Nothing — read-only | Flagging incorrect parameters to the data team so they can be corrected in MDM system |
| **FMS** | Field supply signals as surfaced inside Planning Software | Nothing — no direct FMS access | Flagging stale or implausible field supply signals for the FMS or field operations team to investigate |

## Planning Software: The Primary Working Environment

For a supply planner, Planning Software is the system where the job actually happens. Everything else — ERP, MDM system, FMS — feeds into it or is checked against it.

The working day typically starts at the exception list. Exceptions are alerts the system has raised since the last batch run overnight: a purchase order that was due to be confirmed by the supplier but still shows as unconfirmed, a product that is projected to breach its minimum inventory level within the next two weeks, a production order that has been delayed and needs to be rescheduled in the plan. Each exception is a decision waiting to be made, and clearing the exception list is not a mechanical task — it requires judgment about urgency, commercial impact, and what action is actually available.

For a late purchase order, the planner contacts the supplier to confirm the new expected date, updates the planning system with the revised arrival, and checks whether that delay is short enough to absorb within existing stock or serious enough to escalate. For a projected stockout, the options are narrower than they look: expediting an existing order is only possible if the supplier has the capacity and the lead time allows it, raising a new order only helps if there is time for it to arrive, and adjusting downstream commitments is a conversation with sales or customer service. Planning Software surfaces the problem; the supply planner decides which lever to pull, if any is available.

Planned orders are the other major daily interaction. Planning Software generates these automatically when the planning engine runs — they are recommendations for when to order, how much to order, and from which source. The supply planner reviews them before releasing them, because the system's recommendation is only as good as the parameters it was given. Does the quantity make sense given what the planner knows about this supplier's pallet configuration? Is the requested date achievable given current lead times, or has something changed that the system doesn't yet know about? Are there two small orders for the same supplier within three days of each other that could be consolidated into one shipment? Once the planner is satisfied, they release the planned orders — and those released orders push into ERP as purchase orders or production orders for the execution teams to act on.

Scenario simulation is less frequent than exception management, but it carries more weight. When a significant supply disruption arises — a key supplier announces they cannot deliver for the next six weeks, a production constraint cuts available capacity by thirty percent — the planner doesn't just update the plan directly. They first run a what-if scenario in Planning Software: change the affected assumption, run the planning engine against that altered picture, and compare the simulated plan against the baseline. The output is specific and actionable — which products are affected and by how much, which customer commitments are now at risk, what the earliest realistic recovery date looks like across the portfolio. That analysis is what goes into the supply risk section of the S&OP pack, and it's what gives the executive team enough information to make an informed decision about whether to accelerate alternatives, absorb the impact, or accept the customer service consequences.

The S&OP rhythm is the monthly cadence that sits above all of this. Once a month, the supply planner consolidates the planning picture into the formal supply review: constrained volume by product family, inventory projections across the planning horizon, capacity utilisation rates, and the supply risks that need to be escalated to the executive review. That pack is built from Planning Software data, and producing it is one of the few times the planner steps back from exception-by-exception management to look at the full picture.

## ERP: Read to Reconcile, Write to Release

The supply planner's relationship with ERP is built around a specific tension: Planning Software tells them what the plan says is happening; ERP tells them what is actually happening. When those two views agree, the planner can trust the plan. When they diverge, there is a reconciliation problem to diagnose before the next planning run.

A common scenario: Planning Software shows 500 units of a product on hand, but ERP shows 420. The 80-unit gap needs an explanation before the planner can act on the inventory projection with any confidence. The first check is whether a goods receipt has been posted in ERP for the delivery that arrived this morning. If it has, the question becomes why it hasn't fed into Planning Software yet — which is usually a batch run timing issue that will resolve itself by the next system sync. If the goods receipt hasn't been posted yet, the question is why the warehouse hasn't confirmed receipt, and that is a conversation with the warehouse team, not a planning problem. Knowing the difference between a timing lag and a genuine discrepancy is something that takes experience — and it requires being comfortable navigating both systems.

Purchase order status is another regular read. The supply planner monitors open POs in ERP to track whether suppliers have acknowledged the orders, whether confirmation quantities match the planned quantities, and whether expected arrival dates are being honoured. ERP is the system of record for all of this; Planning Software only reflects what has been communicated back to it through the integration. If a supplier has silently updated an order date in ERP but that change hasn't yet propagated to Planning Software, the planner might be looking at two different pictures of the same order — which is why cross-checking matters.

The primary write action in ERP is order release. When the supply planner approves and releases a planned order in Planning Software, ERP receives it as a purchase order (for externally sourced supply) or a production order (for internally manufactured supply). At that point, ERP owns the order — the execution teams use ERP to manage confirmations, track progress, post receipts, and handle any changes. The supply planner monitors those orders from ERP but doesn't manage their day-to-day execution. The line between supply planning and execution runs roughly at the point of release.

## MDM System: The Source of Planning Parameters

The supply planner never enters data into MDM system. But MDM system data shapes every recommendation Planning Software makes, which means a supply planner who doesn't understand MDM will find themselves constantly confused by the plan.

Lead times determine when the system tells them to order. Lot sizes determine how much the system suggests ordering at once. Safety stock targets set the minimum inventory level the plan tries to maintain. Bills of materials determine which components a production order will consume, and therefore which raw material supply needs to be co-ordinated with finished goods planning. All of these parameters live in MDM system and flow into Planning Software through a regular sync. When a Planning Software recommendation looks wrong, the first diagnostic step is to check the underlying MDM parameter, not to assume the planning logic is broken.

A lot size of 1000 units when the supply planner knows this supplier ships on 500-unit pallets is a data error in MDM system, not a planning algorithm failure. A lead time of five days when the actual transit from this supplier's facility has always been twelve days means the plan is systematically ordering too late — and the only permanent fix is correcting the parameter in MDM system, not repeatedly compensating for it manually in the plan. A safety stock target set two years ago before the business shifted to a more volatile demand pattern may be generating false confidence about inventory coverage.

The supply planner doesn't fix these problems directly. The process is: identify the discrepancy between what MDM system says and what operational reality is, raise it with the data management team with enough specifics that they can investigate and correct it, and then wait for the change to propagate through the next MDM-to-Planning-Software sync before expecting the plan to reflect the correction. This handoff is important — trying to compensate for an MDM error by manually overriding values in Planning Software creates a second, competing source of truth and makes the plan harder to trust.

## FMS: Indirect Signal, Not a Direct Tool

Supply planners in agricultural and field-sourced commodity contexts carry a planning variable that most supply planners don't have to manage: field supply. How much crop is contracted, how the growing season is progressing, what the expected harvest volumes are and when they will be available — all of this shapes the supply plan in ways that have nothing to do with suppliers, production orders, or lead times. It is fundamentally uncertain, seasonally driven, and can change quickly when conditions shift.

All of this originates in the FMS, but the supply planner doesn't log into it. The FMS-to-Planning-Software integration brings field supply signals into the planning model as supply orders or supply signals — the same planning interface shows them alongside purchase orders and production orders, distinguished by their origin type but managed through the same exception and planned order workflow. From the supply planner's perspective, a field supply signal is just another source of supply to plan around.

What does require attention is when those signals look implausible. A crop yield estimate that hasn't been updated in three weeks during a period when weather conditions have changed significantly, or a harvest timing signal that still shows the original contracted date despite a well-known delay in the region — these are FMS data quality issues that will quietly distort the supply plan if they aren't caught. The supply planner is often the first person to notice, because they are the one looking at the full supply picture across all sources. But the resolution goes to the FMS team or field operations team who manage the underlying FMS data — not to the supply planner to fix. The supply planner's role is to flag it clearly, explain the planning implication of the stale data, and follow up until the correction is made and reflected in Planning Software.
