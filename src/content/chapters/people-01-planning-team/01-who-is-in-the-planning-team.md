## Planning is a team sport

A planning system doesn't run itself. Behind every forecast, every supply plan, and every S&OP decision is a group of people deciding what the numbers mean and what to do about them. The technology matters, but the people are what turn data into decisions — and they are usually the hardest part of any planning transformation to get right.

It's easy to underestimate this. New tools get bought, processes get redesigned, dashboards get built — and then the same people are asked to work in a fundamentally different way overnight. Systems can be configured in weeks. Changing how people think, what they own, and how they trust each other takes far longer. That's why most planning programmes that struggle don't fail on the software; they fail on the people side.

## Who owns supply chain planning?

Ownership rarely sits with one person. Planning is inherently cross-functional — it lives at the intersection of commercial, operations, and finance. Someone has to hold the whole thing together, but no single function can run it alone.

In practice the team splits into two layers:

**The core group** does the day-to-day planning work:

- **Demand planners** own the forecast — the statistical baseline plus the market intelligence layered on top.
- **Supply planners** turn that demand signal into a feasible supply response, working within capacity, inventory, and lead-time constraints.
- **The S&OP lead** owns the end-to-end cycle, facilitates the cross-functional reviews, and drives the team to a single agreed plan.

**The extended team** provides essential input but doesn't live in the plan day to day:

- **Finance** validates that the volume plan ties back to the financial plan and the P&L.
- **Commercial** brings the voice of the market — customer commitments, promotions, and new product launches.
- **IT** keeps the platform, integrations, and data pipelines running.

## How the roles interact

The plan flows through these roles in a loop. Commercial and demand planning shape the demand signal; supply planning responds; finance reconciles the result; and the S&OP lead brings everyone together to agree one number. When a role is missing or unclear, the loop breaks — forecasts go unchallenged, supply risks go unescalated, or the financial reality never enters the conversation.

Understanding who does what, and where the handoffs happen, is the foundation for everything else in this chapter.
