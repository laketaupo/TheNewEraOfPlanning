## Why ownership matters

A KPI without an owner is a statistic. An owner is the person accountable for the KPI's performance — not just the person who reports it, but the person who is expected to explain deviations, propose actions, and track improvement.

KPI ownership should sit at the level where the actions that drive the KPI are controlled. OTIF is owned by the supply chain or operations function because they control fulfilment. Forecast accuracy is owned by the commercial or demand planning function because they control the forecast input. Assigning ownership to someone who cannot affect the outcome is a governance failure.

## KPI ownership map

| KPI | Primary Owner | Supporting Owner |
|---|---|---|
| OTIF | Supply Chain / Operations | Planning |
| Forecast accuracy (MAPE) | Demand Planning / Commercial | Planning |
| Forecast bias | Demand Planning / Commercial | Planning |
| Inventory Days on Hand | Planning / Supply Chain | Finance |
| Excess & Obsolete inventory | Planning | Commercial |
| Plan adherence | Planning | Operations |

## Review cadence

KPIs should be reviewed at the appropriate level of the planning cadence:

**Monthly — S&OP cycle**
- Primary KPIs: OTIF, forecast accuracy, inventory DoH, E&O value
- Review format: trend chart (last 6 months), root cause for deviations, actions agreed
- Owner presents; leadership challenges and approves actions

**Weekly — S&OE cycle**
- Operational KPIs: short-horizon OTIF at risk, exception count by type
- Leading indicators only — not a historical review but a forward-looking status check

**Quarterly — strategic review**
- Full KPI framework assessment: are the right metrics being tracked, are targets still appropriate, are the measurement definitions consistent with industry benchmarks?

## KPI targets as policy decisions

Setting a KPI target is a policy decision, not a technical exercise. An OTIF target of 95% commits the organisation to a level of investment in planning, inventory, and execution capability. Targets should be set in S&OP and approved at leadership level — not assigned unilaterally by the planning team. And they should be reviewed annually: a target that was aspirational three years ago may now be business-as-usual, and should be raised.
