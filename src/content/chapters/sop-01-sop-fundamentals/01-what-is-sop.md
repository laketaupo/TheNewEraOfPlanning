## The core purpose of S&OP

Sales & Operations Planning (S&OP) is a structured monthly process that brings together demand, supply, inventory, and financial plans into one agreed-upon view of the business. Its purpose is alignment: ensuring that sales commitments are backed by supply capacity, that financial targets are grounded in operational reality, and that cross-functional decisions are made together rather than in silos.

S&OP operates at an **aggregate level** — typically by product family, region, and month. It is not a day-to-day execution process. It is a planning horizon process that sets the constraints and priorities within which execution happens.

## Why S&OP exists

Without S&OP, each function optimises for its own goals. Sales chases revenue. Supply chases cost efficiency. Finance chases budget adherence. These goals conflict. S&OP creates a forum where conflicts are surfaced, debated, and resolved through explicit decisions rather than silent workarounds.

The output of S&OP is a **consensus plan** — a set of numbers that every function has agreed to live by for the coming period. This plan becomes the reference point for all downstream planning: inventory targets, procurement commitments, capacity investments, and financial forecasts.

## What S&OP produces

A well-run S&OP cycle produces:

- A **consensus demand plan** — the agreed view of what the business expects to sell
- A **supply plan** — the capacity and inventory commitment needed to fulfil demand
- **Resolved trade-offs** — explicit decisions about gaps, constraints, and priorities
- A **financial bridge** — a reconciliation between the operational plan and the financial budget
- **Actions and owners** — decisions assigned with accountability and timing

## S&OP is a decision process, not a meeting

A common misconception is that S&OP is a set of meetings. Meetings are the visible mechanism, but the real work happens in the data preparation, gap analysis, and scenario modelling that feeds each review. The meetings exist to make decisions. If decisions are being deferred or re-litigated every month, the process is failing.
