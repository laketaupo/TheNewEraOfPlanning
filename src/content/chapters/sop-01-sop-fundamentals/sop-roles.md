## The S&OP process owner

Every S&OP process needs an owner — typically the VP of Supply Chain or COO, supported by a dedicated S&OP manager or IBP leader. The process owner is responsible for the calendar, the data quality, the meeting facilitation, and the follow-up on decisions. Without a clear owner, S&OP becomes a series of disconnected reviews with no accountability for outcomes.

## Demand-side participants

The demand review is led by the commercial organisation:

- **Sales** — provides customer-level insights, large order visibility, and win/loss intelligence
- **Marketing** — owns promotion plans, new product launches, and campaign timing
- **Demand planning** — manages the statistical baseline and reconciles commercial adjustments into a single number
- **Finance** — validates that the demand plan aligns with revenue targets and flags material gaps

The output from demand-side participants is a single consensus number per planning bucket — not a range of opinions.

## Supply-side participants

The supply review is led by operations:

- **Manufacturing / production planning** — assesses capacity against the consensus demand plan
- **Procurement** — evaluates supplier lead times, commitments, and material availability
- **Logistics** — assesses distribution capacity, warehousing, and transportation constraints
- **Inventory management** — tracks current stock levels, projected coverage, and reorder points

## Executive participants

The executive S&OP involves the senior leadership team: CEO, CFO, CSO, COO, and relevant business unit heads. Their role is to make the decisions that operational teams cannot — trade-offs between revenue, cost, service level, and capital.

## The S&OP manager role

In organisations running planning software, the S&OP manager plays a critical technical role: maintaining the planning model, ensuring data integrity, preparing scenario analyses for each review, and publishing the agreed plan back into operating systems. This role bridges the process and the technology.

## Common failure: missing the right people

A demand review without sales is a statistics exercise. An executive S&OP without the CFO is a supply chain meeting. S&OP fails when key decision-makers delegate attendance to people who cannot commit the business. Escalation rights — who can override whom — must be explicit in the process design.
