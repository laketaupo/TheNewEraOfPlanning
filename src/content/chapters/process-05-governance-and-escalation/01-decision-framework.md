## Why a decision framework is necessary

In any planning organisation, decisions are made continuously: what to produce, how much safety stock to carry, which customer to prioritise, whether to accept a demand signal at face value. Without a framework, these decisions default to whoever has the most information, the most authority, or the most urgency — which is inconsistent and often wrong.

A decision framework makes explicit what was previously implicit. It documents: this category of decision is made by the planner, this category goes to the planning manager, this category requires S&OP sign-off.

## The three dimensions of a decision

For each significant planning decision, the framework should specify:

**Who decides** — the individual or role with the authority to make the call. This should be the lowest level of the organisation that has enough information and accountability to make a good decision. Elevating decisions unnecessarily slows the process; pushing them down without sufficient context causes errors.

**Within what parameters** — the boundaries within which the decision can be made autonomously. A planner might be authorised to adjust safety stock by ±10% without approval; larger adjustments require sign-off. Defining the parameters allows most decisions to be made quickly without escalation.

**With what information** — the minimum data or analysis required before the decision is made. This prevents decisions made on incomplete information from entering the plan as facts.

## Typical decision tiers

**Planner-level decisions** (operational, reversible, within parameters)
- Adjusting order quantities within ±X% of the plan
- Changing replenishment timing by ±Y days
- Flagging exceptions for review

**Planning manager / S&OE decisions** (short-term, customer-facing, requires trade-off)
- Reprioritising orders within an agreed allocation framework
- Approving exceptions outside planner parameters
- Short-term capacity adjustments

**S&OP / leadership decisions** (strategic, financial impact, cross-functional)
- Changing service level targets
- Approving allocation policy overrides
- Committing to additional supply capacity
- Accepting a planned service level miss

## Keeping the framework current

A decision framework written once and left on a shared drive provides no value. It should be reviewed in the S&OP cycle — at minimum annually — and updated when the organisational structure, planning system, or business context changes. The test of a live decision framework: does the planning team actually refer to it when a decision arises?
