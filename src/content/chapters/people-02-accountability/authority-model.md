## Two Kinds of Authority

Not all planning decisions are the same. The first kind is authority to act *within* a plan — making routine calls inside the boundaries that the plan has already set. A supply planner releasing a purchase order within an agreed quantity range is acting within authority. A demand planner adjusting a short-term forecast inside a tolerance band is doing the same. These decisions belong to the person doing the work, and they do not require sign-off.

The second kind is authority to *change* the plan itself — adjusting targets, overriding constraints, or committing to actions that fall outside what was agreed in the last S&OP cycle. This is a different category entirely. These decisions have knock-on effects across functions and need to be owned at the right level.

Confusing the two is where most authority problems begin. When planners escalate routine acts-within-authority because nobody ever clarified that they were empowered to make them, meetings slow down and decision-making bottlenecks at management. When people change the plan without the authority to do so, commitments made in the executive S&OP quietly unravel at the operational level.

## Setting Decision Thresholds

An authority model works by attaching thresholds to decision types. A threshold defines the point at which a decision must be escalated rather than resolved locally. Common threshold dimensions in planning include:

- **Volume or value** — orders above a quantity or cost limit need approval from the next level up
- **Time horizon** — changes to the frozen zone (typically the nearest 4–6 weeks of plan) require escalation; changes beyond it do not
- **Deviation from plan** — a forecast revision that exceeds a defined percentage of the baseline needs sign-off; smaller revisions do not
- **Cross-functional impact** — any action that requires another function to change their own plan must be agreed jointly, not decided unilaterally

These thresholds do not need to be elaborate. A single page covering the main decision types in each S&OP stage, with the associated threshold and the name of the person who holds authority above that threshold, is enough to operate from.

## What Delegation Looks Like

Delegation is how authority flows downward without creating accountability gaps. The person who holds authority for a decision can delegate that authority — but only the authority, not the accountability. The Accountable party in the RASCI stays fixed; what changes is who is empowered to make the call on their behalf within defined limits.

Effective delegation has three elements:

1. **Explicit scope** — the delegatee knows exactly which decisions are covered and which are not. Broad verbal delegations ("just use your judgement") are not authority — they are abdication.
2. **Clear limits** — the threshold below which the delegatee acts independently and above which they escalate must be stated, not assumed.
3. **Closed loop** — the delegating party stays informed. Delegation is not a way to disappear from a decision domain; it is a way to keep the process moving while maintaining oversight.

## Where This Breaks Down

Authority gaps appear in three predictable places: at the boundary between S&OP and S&OE (who can authorise a within-cycle plan change?), at the handoff between functions (who decides when supply cannot meet a committed demand number?), and at the senior level when the Accountable person is unavailable and no deputy has been named.

The fix in each case is the same — define in advance who makes the call, at what threshold, and who covers when the primary authority holder is absent. These decisions are cheapest to make in a planning session, not in the middle of a crisis.
