## The Problem with Ad-Hoc Prioritisation

When supply is constrained, someone has to decide which customer gets the available stock and which has to wait. In many organisations, that decision falls to whichever planner or customer service representative is handling the account at the time. This feels pragmatic — the person closest to the situation makes the call. In practice, it is one of the most damaging patterns in supply chain execution.

Ad-hoc prioritisation produces inconsistent outcomes. Two customers with the same contractual terms receive different treatment depending on who handled the call, which manager applied pressure, and what time of day the shortage was identified. Over time, customers learn that the squeaky wheel gets the stock — and they behave accordingly, escalating every shortage regardless of severity. The result is more noise, more escalations, and more time spent on internal negotiation rather than actual supply management.

## The Cost of Commercial Override

The most common failure mode is the commercial override: a salesperson calls the supply planner directly to request priority for a specific customer, bypassing any agreed allocation logic. These overrides are rarely documented, rarely reviewed, and rarely challenged. But they carry real costs.

When a high-margin, low-relationship account is consistently prioritised over a strategically important key account because its sales team is more vocal, the business is eroding the relationships it most needs to protect. When a planner approves an override to avoid conflict, they are taking on personal accountability for a decision that should sit at a policy level. When overrides are invisible — not recorded, not reviewed — management has no way to see the pattern or evaluate whether the decisions were right.

## What Policy Does

A clear prioritisation policy shifts the decision from the planner to the system. The policy defines, in advance, how competing orders will be ranked when supply is insufficient to fill all of them. It assigns those rules to the planning system's allocation engine. When a shortage occurs, the system applies the policy — not the person.

This does not eliminate judgement from supply chain execution. There will always be cases that fall outside the policy's scope, and those need human escalation and decision. But for the majority of constraint situations — which follow recognisable patterns — policy eliminates the need for case-by-case decisions, reduces planner fatigue from constant conflict, and creates a consistent, auditable record of who received what and why.

## Planner Fatigue and Decision Quality

There is a human cost to ad-hoc prioritisation that is easy to overlook. Planners who are responsible for allocation decisions under constrained conditions face constant pressure from multiple directions: commercial teams, operations, customer service, and management. Each decision carries risk — the wrong call generates a complaint, an escalation, or a lost customer. Repeated across dozens of constraint events every week, this pressure degrades decision quality over time.

Policy removes most of this burden. The planner's role shifts from making the prioritisation decision to implementing the policy and managing exceptions. That is a sustainable working model. Making high-stakes allocation decisions from scratch, under pressure, every time supply falls short is not.

The goal is a system where the policy is clear, the rules engine is configured to enforce it, and the planner's role is to handle the cases the policy could not anticipate — not to re-litigate the policy every time demand exceeds supply.
