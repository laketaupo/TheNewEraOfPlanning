## Why cadence matters in S&OE

S&OE is a high-frequency process. It runs every week, which means the overhead of each cycle must be low and the structure must be tight. A weekly process that takes three days to complete leaves no time to act on decisions. A process without a fixed schedule drifts — reviews get skipped, exceptions pile up, and execution teams lose the planning signal they need.

## A typical weekly S&OE calendar

The exact timing varies by business, but a common structure looks like this:

**Monday — Exception review and prioritisation**
Planners review the exception queue generated by planning software: what has changed since last week, what alerts have fired, what orders are at risk. The team agrees on priorities for the week.

**Tuesday — Demand execution review**
The demand team reviews the near-term forecast against confirmed orders. Are this week's shipments on track? Are there customer orders that exceed the available plan? Are there demand signals suggesting a change in the coming weeks?

**Wednesday — Supply execution review**
Operations reviews the production schedule and inbound supply against the near-term demand picture. Where is supply short? What needs to be expedited? What can be de-prioritised to free capacity?

**Thursday — Integrated S&OE review**
The demand and supply teams meet jointly to align on the week's plan: agreed priorities, confirmed supply decisions, and a list of items requiring escalation to S&OP. This is the equivalent of the pre-S&OP in the monthly cycle.

**Friday — Plan publication**
The agreed short-term plan is confirmed: replenishment orders released, production schedules locked for the near term, exception resolutions communicated to execution teams.

## The role of planning software in the cadence

Planning software makes the weekly cadence feasible by automating the data preparation. Exception queues are pre-calculated. The constrained supply plan is re-run automatically. Planners arrive at each review with a current picture rather than spending the morning assembling spreadsheets.

## Flexibility within the structure

The weekly calendar is a template, not a rigid script. In practice, businesses adjust based on their supply chain rhythm: manufacturing lead times, supplier delivery patterns, customer order cycles. The key is that the structure exists and is followed consistently — not that it matches a textbook exactly.
