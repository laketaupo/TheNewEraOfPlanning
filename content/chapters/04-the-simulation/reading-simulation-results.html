The comparison view

The primary way to read simulation results is through comparison: the scenario plan vs. the baseline plan, side by side. Planning software provides views that let you see the delta — what changed as a result of your input modification — rather than just the absolute numbers in the new plan.

Focus first on the metrics you noted before the run: total shortage volume, inventory DoH, planned order quantities for the affected items. These are your anchors. The comparison should answer your original question directly.

Key output panels

Inventory projection — the chart showing projected stock over time for affected items. Look for new stock-out periods (inventory hitting zero), new inventory peaks (excess building up), or shifts in the timing of existing events.

Planned orders — the list of new, changed, or cancelled planned orders generated by the scenario. A supply constraint scenario will typically cancel or reduce planned orders; a demand upside scenario will generate new or larger orders.

Exception list — the exceptions flagged in the scenario that did not exist in the baseline. New shortage exceptions indicate where the supply plan cannot cover the changed demand or supply. New excess exceptions indicate over-supply under the new assumptions.

Network waterfall (if available) — the flow of material through the network, showing where constraints bind. This is particularly useful for identifying which node in the supply chain is the bottleneck under the new assumptions.

Interpreting the results

Ask three questions about the output:

  1. Is the magnitude of the impact significant? A 1% change in OTIF risk is probably noise. A 10% increase in total shortage volume is a real signal. Know your materiality threshold before you start interpreting.

  2. Where is the impact concentrated? An end-to-end simulation will show impacts across many items. Focus on the items and time periods where the impact is largest — these are the ones that require a decision.

  3. What is the decision implication? The simulation only has value if it connects to a recommendation. "The simulation shows that under a 25% demand upside, Variety X will be in shortage from week 14. We recommend pre-building stock in weeks 10–12 if the commercial signal strengthens."

Common interpretation mistakes

Cherry-picking — focusing only on the exception items that support the story you want to tell, ignoring items where the simulation shows no impact. Report the full picture.

Confusing the scenario with the forecast — the simulation tests an assumption; it does not mean that assumption is likely. Clearly communicate the conditional nature: "if demand increases 25%, then..." not "demand will increase 25% and therefore..."
