Two escalation thresholds

Demand monitoring in S&OE operates with two distinct escalation thresholds, not one. The first threshold triggers escalation within S&OE — the deviation is too large to absorb silently, so it goes to the integrated S&OE review for a cross-functional decision. The second threshold triggers escalation beyond S&OE into S&OP — the deviation is large enough, persistent enough, or structurally significant enough that a monthly plan revision is needed.

Conflating these two thresholds is a common error. Not every demand deviation that warrants attention in S&OE needs to go to S&OP. The S&OE planner's job is to resolve what can be resolved within the near-term plan and to escalate only what cannot.

The decision logic

The central question the S&OE planner answers when evaluating a demand exception is: can this deviation be absorbed within the current plan?

Absorption is possible when available stock, near-term production capacity, or scheduled inbound supply can cover the demand gap without compromising other customer commitments. If the deviation is upward (demand higher than plan) and available-to-promise inventory is sufficient, the plan can accommodate the additional demand without a formal revision.

Absorption is not possible when:

  • Demand is materially above plan and available supply is already committed to other orders.
  • Demand is materially below plan and production already running cannot be economically deferred or redirected.
  • The deviation has persisted for multiple consecutive weeks, indicating the plan itself is structurally wrong.
  • The deviation affects a high-priority customer, a constrained material, or a product with long replenishment lead times.

When absorption is not possible, the planner escalates. The choice of escalation path — S&OE review or S&OP — depends on whether the response requires near-term operational adjustment alone, or a revision to the baseline plan that only the S&OP cycle can authorise.

S&OE review escalation

When a demand deviation is escalated to the integrated S&OE review, the planner prepares a brief summary covering: the nature of the deviation, the affected items and volumes, the supply position against those items, and the proposed options for resolution. The review meeting then makes a decision — adjust the near-term plan, absorb the deviation, or escalate further to S&OP.

This is a same-week decision. The integrated S&OE review exists precisely to resolve these situations quickly, without waiting for the next monthly S&OP cycle.

S&OP escalation

When the deviation exceeds what S&OE can resolve — because it is large enough to require a plan revision, because it signals a structural shift in demand, or because it requires resource commitments that are outside the S&OE planner's authority — it is formally escalated to S&OP.

The escalation record should include: the deviation data with trend context, the assessment of why this cannot be absorbed within the current plan, and a recommended action for the S&OP team to consider. This becomes an agenda item for the next S&OP demand review, or may trigger an out-of-cycle S&OP meeting if the situation is urgent.

Documenting and communicating the escalation

Every escalation should be logged in the planning system with a clear record of the observation, the decision logic, and the escalation destination. This documentation serves two purposes: it creates accountability for the decision, and it ensures the receiving team (S&OE review or S&OP) has the context they need to act without requiring the planner to repeat the analysis verbally.

The escalation note should answer three questions: what changed, why it matters, and what response is needed.
