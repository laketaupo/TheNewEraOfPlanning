How Planning Software Automates Prioritisation

Modern planning software does not allocate stock manually. It applies a configured rules engine to the open order book whenever inventory is updated, and distributes available supply to open orders according to the policy criteria. This happens automatically — the system does not wait for a planner to review each constraint and make a decision.

The rules engine typically works in two passes. In the first pass, pre-allocated orders are confirmed: orders that have already been committed to specific stock, locked for despatch, or flagged as priority by a previous exception process. In the second pass, remaining available stock is distributed across open unallocated orders according to the priority ranking — customer tier, criticality, contractual obligations, order age — until stock is exhausted or all orders are filled.

Orders that cannot be fully filled after the rules engine runs surface as exceptions. These are not failures in the system — they are the intended output. The system is telling the planner: here are the orders that the policy could not fill. What should we do?

Exception Surfacing and the Planner's Role

The planning system's exception list is the execution planner's primary work queue during a constrained period. Each exception represents an order that the allocation policy could not resolve automatically, and therefore requires human review.

Some exceptions are straightforward: a customer has an order that misses the current run because stock ran out after higher-tier orders were filled. The correct action is to communicate the revised delivery date to the customer and update the order. No policy override is needed.

Other exceptions are more complex: a large commercial over-ride request has arrived, a life-critical order has materialised that was not in the original order book, or an inbound receipt has been short-shipped and multiple tier-1 customers are now competing for reduced supply. These are the cases that require escalation — they involve trade-offs that cannot be resolved within the existing policy.

When to Escalate to S&OE

Escalation to S&OE is appropriate when the exception cannot be resolved within existing policy, or when the trade-off involves a decision above the execution planner's authority. The decision to override customer tier, to break a contractual delivery term, or to prioritise one strategic account over another is not an execution decision — it belongs in the S&OE review.

When raising an escalation, the planner should provide: the specific orders affected and their current allocation status, the reason the policy could not resolve the conflict, the options available (which order is filled, which is deferred, whether partial allocation is possible), the commercial and contractual implications of each option, and a recommended course of action with rationale.

An escalation framed this way gives the S&OE review what it needs to make a fast, informed decision. An escalation framed as "we have a stock shortage, what should we do?" does not.

Documenting Out-of-Policy Decisions

Any decision to override the allocation policy — regardless of the reason — must be documented. The documentation should record: which order was prioritised over policy, the reason given, who authorised the override, and the outcome for the orders that were displaced.

This is not bureaucracy. It is the mechanism that allows management to review whether the policy is working, identify patterns in override requests (which may indicate a policy gap), and hold the business accountable for the commercial consequences of decisions made under constraint. Without it, overrides are invisible, unauditable, and tend to accumulate into a de facto alternative policy that nobody agreed to and nobody can review.
