A Matrix Is Not the Goal

Plenty of organisations build a RASCI, paste it into a slide, and never look at it again. The document is not the point — the behaviour it drives is. A matrix that nobody references during the actual planning cycle is just decoration. Treat it as a working tool, not an artefact.

Common Failure Modes

Three patterns sink most RASCI efforts:

  • Everyone is Responsible for everything. When a column is a wall of Rs, accountability has been spread so thin it no longer exists. If everyone owns the step, nobody truly does, and work falls through the gaps.
  • No single Accountable. Either a step has no A at all, leaving it ownerless, or it has two, which guarantees finger-pointing when something goes wrong. Every step needs exactly one A — no more, no less.
  • Defined but ignored in meetings. The matrix says the Demand Planner is Consulted, yet the review proceeds without their input. Once the RASCI and the real behaviour diverge, the matrix loses all authority and the team stops trusting it.

Four Steps to Make It Stick

  1. Review it with the team regularly. Walk through the matrix at the start of an implementation and revisit it every few cycles. Shared understanding only forms when people see it together and agree out loud who owns what.
  2. Use it to resolve meeting conflicts. When two people both think a decision is theirs, pull up the RASCI. The person marked A makes the call. This turns the matrix from a poster into a live arbitration tool.
  3. Start simple and evolve. Do not try to map every micro-task on day one. Begin with the major steps of each S&OP stage, get those right, and add detail only where ambiguity is actually causing friction.
  4. Hold people to their letter. If someone is Accountable, make them present the outcome. If someone is Consulted, make sure they are genuinely asked. Accountability only becomes real when it shows up in how meetings are run and how decisions are made.

Done well, a RASCI shortens meetings, ends recurring ownership arguments, and makes the whole planning cycle move faster because everyone already knows their part.
