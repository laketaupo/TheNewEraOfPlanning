## The Definition Problem

Most planning teams can point to a RASCI. Far fewer have a RASCI that still reflects how decisions actually get made six months after implementation. The gap is not unusual — it is the default outcome when accountability is treated as something you define once and then leave alone.

Ownership drifts for practical reasons. People change roles. Processes get adjusted without updating the matrix. A shortcut taken during a busy period becomes the new normal. A meeting gets restructured and the Accountable person stops attending. None of these changes are announced; the matrix just quietly stops matching reality.

## Keeping the Matrix Alive

A RASCI needs a review cadence to stay current. This does not require a dedicated governance meeting — a fifteen-minute agenda item at the quarterly S&OP review is usually sufficient. The questions to answer are simple: does the matrix still reflect who is actually making each decision? Have roles changed? Have any new decision types emerged that are not covered?

The review is also the right moment to catch silent drift — cases where the person marked Accountable has effectively handed off the decision without the matrix being updated. When drift is identified, update the matrix immediately. The longer it runs out of date, the less authority it carries, and the less likely anyone is to consult it when a conflict arises.

## Linking Accountability to Meeting Structure

The most effective way to keep ownership real is to make it visible in the meeting structure. If the Demand Review RASCI assigns Accountability to the Demand Manager, then the Demand Manager chairs the review and presents the output. If the Supply Review marks the Supply Planner as Responsible, the Supply Planner does not send a deputy unless one has been formally named.

This connection between the RASCI and who shows up, who presents, and who makes the final call is what turns a document into a living operating model. When the two diverge — when the person in the chair is not the person marked Accountable — the matrix has lost its grip, and accountability has moved to whoever is willing to fill the vacuum.

## Connecting Ownership to KPIs

Accountability without measurement is easy to avoid. Connecting ownership to KPIs closes this gap. If the Demand Manager is Accountable for the forecast, the forecast accuracy KPI should sit in their name, not in a neutral team dashboard. If the Supply Planner owns the constrained supply plan, plan adherence metrics should be reported against that role.

This does not mean punishing individuals for metrics that are genuinely outside their control. It means making sure that the person who owns the outcome can see the result of their decisions and is the one who has to explain variances. Accountability is only real when it carries consequences — including the positive consequence of being visibly credited when things go well.

## Resetting When Ownership Has Drifted

When a RASCI has been ignored long enough that it no longer reflects reality, a reset is more effective than patching. A reset means bringing the relevant people together, walking through the matrix step by step, and agreeing out loud who currently holds each letter. Some changes are legitimate — roles have evolved and the matrix needs to catch up. Others will surface decisions that have been floating without a clear owner, which is usually where the most persistent problems live.

After the reset, build the maintenance habits immediately: schedule the next quarterly review, add the RASCI to the standard agenda of the S&OP cycle, and name a person responsible for keeping it current. A RASCI without a named maintainer will drift again.
