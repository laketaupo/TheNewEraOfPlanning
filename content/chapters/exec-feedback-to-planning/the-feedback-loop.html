The Planning Cycle Is a Loop

The supply chain planning cycle is often described as a cascade: S&OP sets the monthly plan, S&OE manages the near-term week, execution delivers against the daily schedule. That description is accurate as far as it goes — but it describes only one direction of information flow. The cascade goes down. The feedback must go back up.

Without a structured feedback loop from execution to planning, the planning cycle is not a cycle at all. It is a pipeline that produces plans, sends them downstream, and has no mechanism to learn from what actually happened. Each planning cycle starts from the same assumptions as the last, regardless of whether those assumptions held. Over time, the gap between what the plan expects and what execution actually delivers grows — and the plan becomes progressively less useful as a decision-making tool.

Why Feedback Cannot Be Left to Exception Management

Many supply chain teams rely on exception management as their feedback mechanism: when something goes wrong badly enough, someone escalates it, the planner adjusts, and the deviation is absorbed. This works for one-off events. It does not work for systematic patterns.

If a supplier reliably delivers three days after their confirmed lead time, execution will generate an exception every time that supplier's order is due. The S&OE team will manually adjust the supply position every week. The planner will note the pattern mentally. But if no one feeds that pattern back into the planning parameters — if the lead time in the system still shows the contractual term rather than the actual delivery performance — the next planning cycle will generate the same supply gap it always has, and the same exception will be handled again.

Exception management absorbs individual deviations. The feedback loop prevents recurring ones. Both are necessary, but they are not the same thing.

The Three Data Flows That Close the Loop

Three specific data flows connect execution actuals to the planning model and keep the plan aligned with reality.

Shipment actuals deplete the demand plan. When an order is shipped and confirmed, that quantity is consumed against the demand forecast. The planning model updates: the demand that has been met is no longer an open requirement, and the remaining uncovered demand is what the plan must still fulfill. Without this update, the planning model sees total forecast demand as unsatisfied even after deliveries have been made, and drives unnecessary replenishment.

Production actuals update the inventory position. When a production batch completes — with whatever yield was actually achieved — the confirmed output quantity becomes available stock. If yield is below plan, the inventory position is lower than expected and the gap must be assessed against open demand. If yield is above plan, there may be inventory accumulating above target. Either way, the planning model must reflect actual production output, not planned output, to maintain an accurate stock position.

Receipt actuals update the supply position. When a goods receipt is posted, the confirmed inbound quantity updates the available supply position. Short deliveries reduce available supply below plan; early deliveries may bring supply forward in time; rejected receipts remove supply entirely. Each of these updates changes the constraint picture the planner is working with.

When Feedback Is Delayed

When these three data flows are delayed — when shipment confirmations are batched, production confirmations are deferred until end of shift, and goods receipts are posted the morning after delivery — the planning model operates on a lag. The plan that the S&OE team is reviewing at 9am reflects the business as it was at the previous evening's data cut, not as it is now.

For most supply chains, this lag is manageable if it is consistent and understood. Planners know the data is a few hours old and account for it. The problem is when the lag is variable and unpredictable — sometimes a few hours, sometimes a full day, sometimes longer. Variable lag is worse than consistent lag because planners cannot calibrate for it. They do not know whether the data they are seeing is current or stale, which means every decision carries an unquantified data quality risk.

Closing the feedback loop is not just about getting the data to planning. It is about getting it there consistently, on a known schedule, so that the planning model can be trusted as a reliable reflection of where the business is.
