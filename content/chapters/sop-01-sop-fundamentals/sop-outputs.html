The consensus plan

The primary output of S&OP is the consensus plan: a set of agreed volumes, revenue projections, inventory levels, and supply commitments that the business will operate to until the next cycle. The consensus plan is the single version of truth. Every function — sales, operations, finance — should be able to point to the same numbers.

Decision log

Every S&OP review should produce a decision log: a written record of decisions made, trade-offs approved, and issues deferred. The decision log is critical for two reasons:

  1. Accountability — decisions without an owner and a date are not decisions; they are conversations
  2. Continuity — the log creates an auditable trail that prevents the same issue from being relitigated every month

Actions and follow-ups

Decisions generate actions. The executive S&OP ends with a list of actions, owners, and due dates. At the start of the next cycle, the first agenda item is a review of open actions from the previous month. S&OP loses credibility quickly if decisions are made but never followed through.

Financial bridge

A mature S&OP process includes a financial bridge — a reconciliation that shows how the operational plan translates into revenue, cost, and margin. This bridges the gap between the supply chain view (units, volume, coverage) and the finance view (£, margin %). Without this bridge, S&OP and the budget remain two separate processes that never talk to each other.

Updated planning horizons

The agreed plan is published back into planning software and ERP systems. Near-term months become the reference for S&OE and execution. Outer months update inventory targets, procurement commitments, and capacity plans. Publishing the plan is not optional — if downstream processes are not updated, the cycle has not produced anything useful.
