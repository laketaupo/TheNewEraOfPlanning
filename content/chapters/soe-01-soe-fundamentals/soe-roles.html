S&OE is planner-driven

Unlike S&OP, which requires executive participation, S&OE is driven by operational planners. The process must be able to complete — and publish a plan — without waiting for senior management. Decisions that require executive input belong in S&OP, not S&OE.

Core S&OE roles

Demand planner Owns the near-term demand picture. Monitors confirmed orders against the weekly plan, flags deviations, and maintains the short-term forecast. In S&OE, the demand planner is less concerned with the statistical model and more focused on firm orders, customer commitments, and imminent demand changes.

Supply / replenishment planner Owns the near-term supply plan. Manages replenishment orders, monitors inbound deliveries, and works with operations to adjust the production schedule in response to demand or supply changes. Responsible for exception resolution on supply-side alerts.

S&OE coordinator / IBP manager Owns the weekly calendar, facilitates the integrated review, and maintains the escalation log. Ensures the process runs on time, actions are followed up, and items requiring S&OP escalation are packaged correctly.

Operations lead Represents manufacturing and logistics in the weekly supply review. Provides visibility of production constraints, quality holds, and capacity availability for the near-term horizon.

Who does NOT participate in S&OE

S&OE is not an executive process. The CEO, CSO, and CFO do not attend weekly S&OE reviews. Their involvement in operational decisions is a symptom of either:

  • A lack of trust in the S&OE process to resolve issues without escalation
  • Genuine strategic issues that should be resolved in S&OP, not the weekly cycle

If executives are regularly pulled into weekly operational reviews, the S&OP and S&OE process boundaries need to be re-examined.

Customer-facing roles

In businesses with key account relationships, a customer service or account management representative may participate in the demand execution review to provide visibility of customer commitments and early warning of demand changes. This is particularly valuable when the near-term demand plan is driven more by customer orders than statistical signals.
