When S&OE cannot contain a problem

S&OE operates within the guardrails of the S&OP plan. Most deviations — a missed delivery, a demand spike in a single week — can be absorbed through the tactical tools available to planners: expediting, reallocation, schedule adjustment. But some deviations are too large, too persistent, or too strategically significant for the weekly process to handle.

Escalation to S&OP is required when:

  • The deviation is structural — a change in demand or supply that will persist for multiple months, not just one week
  • The financial impact is material — a service level miss that will affect a major customer or a revenue target
  • Operational tools are exhausted — the planner has no remaining levers to close the gap within their authority
  • A decision is needed above planner level — a trade-off between service level, cost, and inventory that requires sign-off from commercial or financial leadership

The escalation package

An escalation from S&OE to S&OP should be a structured package, not a phone call or an email. The integrated S&OE review produces a log of escalation items for each week. Each item should include:

  • What is the problem — the gap, its size, and its projected impact
  • What has already been tried — actions taken in S&OE and why they were insufficient
  • What decision is needed — the specific trade-off or authority required from S&OP
  • Urgency — whether this can wait until the next monthly cycle or requires an extraordinary review

Extraordinary S&OP reviews

Not every escalation can wait for the monthly cycle. Planning organisations should have a defined process for calling an extraordinary S&OP review — a focused session with the necessary stakeholders to make a time-sensitive decision. This should be used sparingly; if it is happening every week, the S&OP cycle itself needs adjustment.

Closing the loop

When S&OP makes a decision in response to an S&OE escalation, that decision must be communicated back to the S&OE team with enough clarity to update the operational plan. Decisions that are made but not translated into updated plans in the system are decisions that have no effect on what actually happens.
