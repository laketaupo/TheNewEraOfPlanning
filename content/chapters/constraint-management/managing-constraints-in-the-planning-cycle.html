Three Strategies for Managing a Constraint

Once a binding constraint is identified, the planning team has three strategic options. These come directly from the Theory of Constraints and map cleanly onto the decisions that different planning forums are equipped to make.

Exploit the constraint. Maximise throughput through the constraint by removing waste and prioritising the right product mix. This means: ensuring the constrained resource is never starved of work, never producing scrap if it can be avoided, running the highest-margin or highest-priority products through the bottleneck first, and eliminating changeovers or downtime that reduce its effective capacity. Exploitation does not require investment — it requires discipline in how the existing constraint is used. It is an S&OE-level action, owned by the operational team and the supply planner.

Subordinate all other activities to the constraint. Align the rest of the supply chain to the constraint's pace. This means: upstream processes should not produce more than the constraint can absorb (to avoid building buffer inventory in the wrong place), downstream processes should not be scheduled as though the constraint will deliver more than it can. Subordination is a scheduling and planning logic change — it requires the planning model to treat the constraint as the pacemaker, not an obstacle to work around. This is a process and planning configuration change, typically owned by the supply planning team.

Elevate the constraint. Invest to increase the constraint's capacity — additional equipment, extra shifts, outsourced production, an alternative supplier. Elevation involves cost and typically requires capital approval or a supplier negotiation. It is a decision made at the S&OP or strategic planning level, not at the operational level. Elevation is not always the right answer: if the constraint is temporary or if demand is uncertain, the investment may not be justified.

The three strategies are sequential in the sense that exploit and subordinate should be exhausted before elevation is considered. It is common for teams to jump straight to elevation ("we need more capacity") without first examining whether the existing constraint is being fully exploited and whether the rest of the system is properly subordinated to it.

How Constraint Decisions Flow Through the Planning Hierarchy

Constraint management is not a single-forum activity. It appears at every level of the planning hierarchy, and different levels make different types of decisions.

S&OE manages constraint exceptions operationally within the current confirmed plan. At this level, decisions are short-horizon and time-critical: which orders to prioritise through the bottleneck today, whether to expedite or reschedule a production run, how to allocate limited stock across competing demand. S&OE does not change the constraint — it manages within it. The primary tools are the exception queue, the production schedule, and direct communication with the operational team.

S&OP makes constrained supply decisions for the rolling planning horizon — typically one to eighteen months. The supply review step of S&OP is where binding constraints are formally reviewed, exploitation and subordination strategies are confirmed, and elevation decisions are tabled for approval. S&OP has the authority and the horizon to make trade-off decisions that S&OE cannot — choosing which product families or customer segments to prioritise when constrained capacity cannot meet all demand.

Strategic planning handles elevation decisions that require capital expenditure, long-term supplier agreements, or structural changes to the production network. These decisions sit outside the monthly S&OP cycle and are owned by senior leadership and finance. The planning team's role in this context is to provide the constraint analysis that justifies the investment case — showing the utilisation trajectory, the lost revenue or margin from the constraint, and the expected return from elevation.

Constraint Management as a Cross-Cycle Capability

Constraint management is not an S&OP activity or an S&OE activity — it is a cross-cycle capability that shows up at every level. The same constraint that is being managed operationally in S&OE this week is being reviewed strategically in S&OP this month and potentially being addressed through capital investment in the annual planning cycle.

This layered view matters for planning teams because it clarifies who owns what. The S&OE team owns exploitation. The S&OP team owns the constrained plan and the elevation decision. The strategic planning team owns the investment. If each forum tries to do all three, decisions are made at the wrong level with the wrong authority and the wrong time horizon.

In practice, good constraint management feels like a relay: S&OE handles the constraint today, S&OP decides what to do about it this quarter, and strategic planning determines whether to remove it permanently. The handoffs between these forums — and the quality of information flowing between them — determine whether the organisation manages its constraints or is managed by them.
