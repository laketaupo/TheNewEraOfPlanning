## The Rhythm Behind the Plan

Planning is not a single event — it is a system of recurring activities happening at different frequencies and different time horizons simultaneously. The operational team is adjusting today's execution. The tactical team is managing this week and next. The strategic team is shaping the next 12–18 months. All three are running at once, each feeding into the others.

What holds this together is cadence: a predictable calendar of when planning activities happen, who is involved, and what decisions get made. Without cadence, each horizon operates in isolation — the monthly plan disconnects from weekly reality, and daily execution drifts from both.

---

## Three Horizons, Three Rhythms

The planning function operates across three horizons, each with its own drumbeat.

**Operational — weekly**

This is the execution horizon: the next 1–4 weeks. Demand and supply signals are reviewed daily or weekly. Exceptions are triaged and resolved. Actuals are captured in ERP. The key process here is S&OE (Sales and Operations Execution), which runs on a weekly cadence and culminates in an integrated review where cross-functional exceptions are resolved or escalated.

**Tactical — monthly**

This is the near-term management horizon: roughly the next 1–3 months. The tactical planner's job is to keep the near-term plan credible — disaggregating the monthly plan from S&OP into executable weekly buckets, managing constraints as they surface, and feeding actuals back upward into the next S&OP cycle. The rhythm here is shaped by the monthly S&OE close and S&OP handover.

**Strategic — quarterly and beyond**

This is the S&OP horizon: typically 12–18 months out, reviewed monthly. Each month, the strategic planner runs the full S&OP cycle — demand review, supply review, pre-S&OP, and executive S&OP — and closes a new monthly plan. The quarter adds a further layer: reviewing how the rolling plan is tracking, running scenario analysis, and feeding into any quarterly business review or IBP process.

---

## How S&OE and S&OP Interlock

S&OE and S&OP are not separate processes — they are two cadences running on the same plan. S&OE monitors and adjusts the near-term execution of the S&OP plan. S&OP reviews what S&OE has surfaced (actuals, exceptions, near-term constraints) and shapes the next planning horizon accordingly.

The information flow is continuous:

- Operational actuals captured daily and weekly flow up to the tactical planner
- The tactical planner curates these into a near-term performance summary that feeds the strategic planner's S&OP reviews
- The strategic planner closes the monthly plan and publishes it — which the tactical planner then disaggregates and executes against

Break any link in that chain and the plan loses contact with reality. The cadence calendar exists to make the handoffs predictable and deliberate.

---

## A Month in the Cadence Calendar

A typical month looks like this:

**Weeks 1–4: S&OE runs continuously**

Each week, the operational and tactical planners run the S&OE cycle: monitoring demand and supply signals, managing exceptions, and conducting the integrated S&OE review. This is the weekly heartbeat.

**Week 3: S&OP input is assembled**

Before the S&OP cycle can run, the tactical planner assembles the near-term performance summary — what moved as planned, what did not, open risks — and submits it to the strategic planner. This is the upward feed.

**Week 4: S&OP runs**

The strategic planner runs the full S&OP sequence: demand review, supply review, pre-S&OP, financial reconciliation, executive S&OP. The month closes with a published plan.

**Week 1 of next month: The cycle resets**

The new monthly plan is disaggregated by the tactical planner and becomes the baseline for the next four weeks of S&OE.

---

## Why Timing Matters

The value of a planning cadence is not just that things happen — it is that they happen in the right order. Demand signals need to be consolidated before the supply review can run. Actuals need to be posted in ERP before the near-term summary can be assembled. The executive S&OP needs the cross-functional pack before it can make decisions.

When the cadence slips — when actuals are late, reviews are skipped, or the S&OP pack arrives incomplete — the downstream processes run on stale information. Plans are made on assumptions that have already moved. Exceptions go unresolved because the review that should have caught them did not happen in time.

A well-run planning cadence is a governance mechanism as much as a process rhythm. It sets the expectation that certain information will be available by certain times, certain decisions will be made in certain forums, and the plan will be updated and re-published on a known schedule. That predictability is what allows everyone — operational, tactical, and strategic — to act in coordination rather than in isolation.
