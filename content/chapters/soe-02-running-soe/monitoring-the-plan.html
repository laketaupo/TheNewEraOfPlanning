The starting point: what does the plan say?

Before planners can manage exceptions, they need a clear picture of the current plan and how actuals are tracking against it. In planning software, this means opening the S&OE dashboard at the start of each week and reviewing:

  • Confirmed orders vs. plan — are this week's orders aligned with the demand plan, above it, or below it?
  • Inventory positions — where does stock stand relative to safety stock targets and projected coverage?
  • Inbound supply — what deliveries are expected this week, and are any flagged as delayed?
  • Production schedule — is manufacturing on track to meet this week's output targets?

The difference between monitoring and reviewing everything

Monitoring is not the same as reviewing every SKU every week. Good S&OE monitoring is exception-driven: planners look at dashboards that highlight deviations from plan, not tables of every item in the supply chain. The system does the pattern recognition; the planner provides the judgment on what to do about it.

Key metrics to track in S&OE

  • Order fill rate — are customer orders being fulfilled at the committed service level?
  • On-time in-full (OTIF) — are deliveries arriving when and in the quantity planned?
  • Forecast accuracy at week level — is the near-term demand signal reliable?
  • Days of stock on hand — is inventory within the target range?
  • Open exception count — how many unresolved exceptions are in the queue, and is the number growing or shrinking?

The role of planning software dashboards

A well-configured S&OE dashboard in planning software surfaces the exceptions and metrics that matter for the current week's cycle. Planners should not have to build reports to get this picture — it should be available immediately when they open the system. If planners are spending the first part of every week assembling data, that is a signal that the system configuration or data infrastructure needs attention.
