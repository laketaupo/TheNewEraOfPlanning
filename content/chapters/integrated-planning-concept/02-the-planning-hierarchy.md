## A hierarchy of decisions

Integrated planning is not a flat structure. It is organised as a hierarchy of four levels, each making decisions at a different level of resolution, over a different time horizon, and with a different cadence. Understanding this hierarchy is essential to understanding why the system works when it works — and why it breaks down when levels are confused or bypassed.

The four levels, from top to bottom, are: strategic plan, Sales & Operations Planning (S&OP), Sales & Operations Execution (S&OE), and execution.

## Strategic plan

At the top of the hierarchy sits the strategic plan. This is typically an annual or multi-year exercise that sets the direction for the business: which markets to compete in, what capacity investments to make, what service and cost targets to achieve.

The strategic plan does not produce a production schedule. It produces boundaries — volume ranges, capital budgets, network configurations — within which the rest of the hierarchy operates. These boundaries change slowly and require senior leadership decisions.

## S&OP

S&OP translates the strategic plan into a monthly rolling plan for the mid-term horizon — typically 3 to 18 months. It balances aggregate demand and supply, sets inventory targets, aligns with the financial plan, and produces a single agreed view of what the business will do.

S&OP operates at an aggregate level: product families, regions, monthly buckets. Its output is not a detailed schedule but a set of constraints and targets that S&OE can work within. It is the primary mechanism by which cross-functional trade-offs are made explicitly rather than left to chance.

Every decision in S&OP is constrained by the strategic plan above it. Every decision S&OE makes must fit within the plan S&OP has set.

## S&OE

S&OE takes the S&OP plan and manages its execution over the near-term horizon — typically zero to twelve weeks. Running weekly, it monitors whether the plan is on track, identifies deviations and risks, and makes the tactical adjustments needed to stay within the boundaries S&OP has set.

S&OE does not rebuild the plan. It works within it, resolving day-to-day variability through exception management and escalating only what genuinely requires a cross-functional or S&OP-level decision. This distinction — what S&OE can resolve versus what must go back to S&OP — is one of the most important design questions in any planning operating model.

## Execution

At the base of the hierarchy is execution: the daily activities of order fulfilment, production scheduling, procurement, and logistics. Execution translates the weekly priorities from S&OE into specific actions on the shop floor, in the warehouse, and with suppliers.

Execution does not plan — it acts. The planning decisions have already been made by the levels above. What execution does is apply discipline, maintain visibility, and communicate deviations quickly enough that S&OE can respond.

## How decisions flow down and actuals flow back up

The hierarchy is not a one-way waterfall. Decisions flow downward, becoming more specific and more constrained at each level. But actuals, deviations, and risks flow back upward.

When execution identifies a supplier failure that cannot be resolved within the current week's plan, it escalates to S&OE. When S&OE identifies a structural gap that cannot be resolved within the S&OP plan, it escalates upward. Actual performance data — shipments, production output, inventory positions — feeds back into S&OP to keep the plan grounded in reality.

This two-way flow is what makes the hierarchy a system rather than a set of disconnected processes.
