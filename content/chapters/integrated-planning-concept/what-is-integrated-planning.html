The core principle

Integrated planning is the discipline of planning demand, supply, inventory, finance, and operations as a single connected system rather than as separate functions that periodically share spreadsheets.

The principle sounds obvious. In practice, most organisations do not do it. Demand planning runs its forecast. Supply planning runs its own numbers. Finance builds a budget from different assumptions. Operations makes its own capacity decisions. Each function is optimising locally — and the organisation as a whole pays the price through excess inventory, missed service targets, and financial surprises.

Integration does not mean everyone agrees all the time. It means decisions made in one part of the plan are immediately visible to, and constrained by, decisions in every other part.

Why siloed planning fails

When planning functions work in isolation, a predictable set of failures emerges.

Demand and supply plans diverge. The commercial team commits to volume that supply cannot support. Supply builds inventory for demand that will not materialise. Neither team knows about the gap until the shortfall or the write-off appears.

Financial and operational plans contradict each other. The budget assumes a cost structure that the supply plan cannot achieve. The S&OP process produces a volume plan that finance has not modelled. Month-end closes become exercises in explaining variances rather than managing the business.

Inventory becomes a buffer for dysfunction rather than a strategic asset. Safety stock is set to cover the uncertainty created by poor handoffs between functions, not genuine market volatility.

What integration actually requires

Integration is not a technology project, though good planning software makes it much easier. It requires three things.

A shared data model. Demand, supply, inventory, and financial plans must live in a connected data structure where a change in one propagates to the others. If the demand planner updates the forecast in one system and the supply planner works from a different one, integration cannot exist.

A shared planning process. Integration requires structured forums — primarily S&OP — where cross-functional decisions are made together rather than being delegated to individual functions. These forums need authority to resolve trade-offs, not just report information.

Agreed single numbers. Integrated planning produces one set of numbers that all functions plan from. Not a commercial forecast and a supply forecast and a finance forecast that are never reconciled. One number, with owned assumptions and a process for updating it.

Integration as a continuous capability

Integrated planning is not a project that gets completed. It is a capability that matures over time. Early on, the goal is simply to get functions looking at the same numbers in the same meeting. Over time, the data becomes more connected, the process becomes more disciplined, and decisions become faster and better.
