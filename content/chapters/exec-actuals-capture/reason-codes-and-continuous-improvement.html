What Reason Codes Are For

A reason code is a short classification attached to an execution deviation at the point of recording. When a supplier delivers short, when a production batch yields below target, when a shipment is delayed, when stock is written off — the system captures not just what happened (the outcome), but why it happened (the cause).

Reason codes are sometimes treated as a compliance requirement: a mandatory field that has to be filled in before the system will save the transaction. Treated that way, they accumulate garbage data — "other", "unknown", "see notes" — that is useless for analysis. The purpose of reason codes is not to satisfy a system requirement. It is to build a structured record of why execution deviated from plan, so the business can identify patterns, assign accountability, and make informed decisions about whether and how to change.

The Three Levels Where Reason Codes Add Value

At the transaction level, a reason code provides context that the quantity alone cannot. A short goods receipt is a number: 480 units against 500 ordered. A reason code — "supplier production shortage", "quality rejection at goods-in", "carrier partial loss" — tells the receiving team and the planner what kind of problem it is, which determines the response. A quality rejection requires a supplier corrective action request. A carrier loss requires a carrier claim. A supplier production shortage may trigger an emergency sourcing discussion. Without the reason code, all three look the same in the system: a short receipt.

At the exception level, reason codes allow the S&OE team to categorise exceptions quickly during their review. If the exception queue shows twelve short receipts, knowing that eight are from the same supplier with "production shortage" tells a different story — and requires a different response — than twelve short receipts from twelve different suppliers with twelve different codes.

At the pattern level, reason code data over time is the input to root cause analysis and planning parameter improvement. A supplier who has accumulated fifteen "production shortage" codes in the past quarter has a structural reliability problem that should be reflected in lead time buffers or safety stock for their items. A production line with recurring "yield loss" codes on a specific product has an engineering or quality issue that should be investigated before the planning team simply absorbs it as a parameter adjustment.

What Good Reason Code Discipline Looks Like

Good reason code discipline means three things. First, codes are assigned at the point of recording, by the person posting the transaction — not added later as an afterthought or by a different team. Second, the code taxonomy is specific enough to be useful (not just "supplier issue", but "supplier production shortage", "supplier quality failure", "supplier logistics delay") and agreed across the teams who use it. Third, codes are reviewed regularly — not stored in a database and ignored — by the S&OE team as part of exception pattern analysis and by supply chain management as part of supplier and operational performance review.

The taxonomy matters more than teams expect. If "other" is an available option, it will be overused. If the codes do not match the real causes that occur in the operation, teams will pick the closest available code rather than the right one, and the data will mislead analysis. Building a good reason code structure requires input from the people who post transactions, not just the planners and analysts who read the reports.

The Feedback Loop to S&OP

The ultimate destination of reason code data is the S&OP planning process. When a systematic execution variance is identified — a supplier reliably delivering 10-15% below ordered quantity, a production line yielding 5% below standard on a specific SKU — that variance should not simply be absorbed by the S&OE team as a recurring exception. It should flow upstream to S&OP as a planning parameter update: adjust the expected yield, build additional buffer into the supplier lead time, review the safety stock policy for items sourced from that supplier.

This feedback loop only works if the execution data is there. Reason codes, captured consistently and classified accurately at the point of the execution event, are what make that data available. Without them, S&OP operates on the assumption that the planning parameters it set last quarter still reflect reality — which, for any supply chain that has been running for more than a few months without review, is unlikely to be true.
