The connection between horizons and cycles

The three planning horizons — long-term, mid-term, and short-term — are not just conceptual boundaries. Each one is actively managed by a process cycle with its own cadence, participants, and outputs. Getting this mapping right is what turns the horizon model from theory into a functioning operating system.

S&OP manages the mid-to-long horizon

Sales & Operations Planning is the cycle that owns the tactical and strategic horizon. Its monthly cadence is suited to decisions that play out over months: aggregate supply and demand balance, inventory strategy, capacity commitments, and financial alignment.

S&OP sets the authoritative plan for roughly 3 to 18 months out. It is the cycle where cross-functional trade-offs are made at an aggregate level — by product family, region, and period. The output is not a detailed schedule; it is a set of agreed constraints and priorities that the next cycle down can work within.

When S&OP is running well, the mid-term horizon is covered. Gaps between demand and supply are visible months before they become crises. Financial and operational plans are reconciled. Escalations happen early enough that options still exist.

S&OE manages the near-term horizon

Sales & Operations Execution is the cycle that owns the operational horizon — roughly zero to twelve weeks. Where S&OP sets the plan, S&OE monitors whether the plan is holding and makes the adjustments needed to stay on track.

S&OE runs weekly. Its participants are operational planners rather than senior leadership, and its focus is exception management: what has changed since the last cycle, what is at risk, and what needs to be escalated to S&OP versus resolved within the existing plan.

S&OE works within the commitments S&OP has made. It does not reconfigure the supply network or rebalance the financial plan — it executes and adapts within the boundary set one level up.

Execution manages the immediate

Daily execution — order fulfilment, production scheduling, logistics coordination — manages the plan in real time. At this level, the planning decisions have already been made. What remains is translating priorities into actions and flagging when reality diverges far enough from the plan to require escalation.

Execution operates inside the firm time fence. Its job is not to replan but to execute with discipline and communicate deviations clearly.

How a decision sharpens as it flows down

Consider a demand increase for a product family six months out. In S&OP, this shows up as a gap between the demand forecast and available supply — a decision is made to authorise additional production in that period. The output is a revised aggregate supply plan.

Six weeks before the period, S&OE picks up that plan and translates it into specific weekly production targets. It monitors supplier confirmations and flags risks. Two weeks out, the execution team receives a firm production schedule. The following Monday, the shop floor runs the schedule.

The same demand signal has flowed from a high-level S&OP decision to a daily execution action. At each step, the plan became more specific, more constrained, and less changeable — exactly as the time fence model predicts.

When the mapping breaks down

The most common failure is when cycles are disconnected. S&OP produces a plan that S&OE never references, so S&OE improvises. Or S&OE makes changes inside the firm fence without authorisation, undermining execution stability.

A clear horizon-to-cycle map prevents this by making explicit which cycle owns which decisions. It is the foundation for designing a planning operating model that actually works.
