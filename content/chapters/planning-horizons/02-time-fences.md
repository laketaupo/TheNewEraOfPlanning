## What a time fence is

A time fence is a boundary within the planning horizon that defines the rules for making changes to the plan. Inside a fence, changes are restricted or prohibited. Outside it, changes are expected and accommodated.

The logic is straightforward: the closer an event is, the more expensive it becomes to change. Shifting a production run scheduled for tomorrow has real cost — materials may already be staged, labour is rostered, and customers may be expecting delivery. Changing a plan for six months from now costs almost nothing. Time fences make this economic reality explicit.

## The three zones

Most planning systems recognise three zones, each with different change rules.

**The firm fence** is the innermost zone — typically zero to four weeks, though this varies by industry and lead time. Inside the firm fence, the plan is frozen. Changes require explicit authorisation from a senior planner or operations manager, and they come with an understood cost. This zone protects the execution team from constant replanning and gives supply chain operations the stability to function efficiently.

**The trade-off fence** is the middle zone — roughly four to twelve weeks. Changes here are possible but require a conscious trade-off. Adding volume requires confirming that capacity exists or that something else will give way. The constraint is real, but it can be negotiated. This is where S&OE operates — monitoring and adjusting within what was agreed in S&OP, escalating where adjustments require cross-functional decisions.

**The open fence** covers everything beyond the trade-off fence. In this zone, the plan is a working assumption, not a commitment. Changes are expected as forecasts update, commercial conditions shift, or strategic priorities evolve. S&OP reviews live here, taking the plan forward and making the assumptions explicit.

## Why zones have different rules

Without time fences, every change request looks the same regardless of timing. Planning teams spend their time reworking schedules that are days away from execution, supply chains oscillate with every forecast update, and operational teams lose confidence in any plan they receive.

Time fences are not bureaucracy — they are a mechanism for absorbing uncertainty at the right level. Long-term uncertainty is absorbed by the open zone through regular plan updates. Medium-term uncertainty is managed through trade-off decisions. Near-term uncertainty is absorbed by safety stock, flex capacity, and exception management — not by replanning.

## Connection to S&OP and S&OE cadence

Time fences do not exist in isolation — they must align with the process cadences that manage each zone. The S&OP monthly cycle updates the plan in the open and trade-off zones. The S&OE weekly cycle monitors and adjusts within the trade-off zone. The execution team manages the firm zone with daily priorities and exception escalation.

When the fence definitions and the process cadences are out of step — for instance, when the S&OE cadence expects to make changes that sit inside the firm fence — conflict and frustration follow. Aligning the fences with the rhythms of S&OP and S&OE is a fundamental configuration decision for any planning organisation.
