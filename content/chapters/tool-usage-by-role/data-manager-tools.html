MDM System: The Primary Working Environment

MDM system is where the data manager does the majority of their hands-on work, and understanding what that work actually involves matters — because it is more complex than "keeping records tidy."

Every product that flows through the supply chain must exist in MDM system as an item master record before it can be planned or transacted. When a new SKU is introduced, a new raw material is qualified, or a new packaging format is approved, the data manager creates the record. When an existing product changes — a revised unit of measure, a new supplier assignment, updated storage conditions — the data manager maintains it. The creation and maintenance work sounds routine, but the fields that matter most are not the commercial ones. Planning parameters embedded in the item master — lead times, minimum order quantities, lot sizes, reorder points, safety stock levels — are what Planning Software reads to generate supply recommendations. If those fields are incomplete or wrong, every recommendation built on them is wrong too.

Bills of Material (BOMs) are among the most planning-critical records the data manager is responsible for. A BOM defines what goes into a finished or semi-finished product: which input materials, in what quantities, with what yield factors. Yield factors are where errors are most consequential. If a BOM specifies that producing one tonne of a finished product requires 1.18 tonnes of a raw material (an 85% yield), but the actual production yield has drifted to 78%, every production order and supply plan built on that BOM will underestimate raw material consumption. The plan looks fine on paper until a production run short-ships because the raw material wasn't available in sufficient quantity. Tracing that back to a stale yield factor in MDM system requires the data manager to have built close enough relationships with production and R&D to get accurate process data, not just approved-on-paper specifications.

Bills of Distribution (BODs) are the other major planning-critical structure. A BOD defines how a finished product flows from production sites through the distribution network — which regional warehouses stock it, how replenishment moves between tiers, and in what direction supply flows. An incorrect BOD causes Planning Software to route supply through the wrong distribution path. A product that should be replenished from Site A into Warehouse East may instead be signalled to replenish from Site B, adding lead time and cost, and potentially drawing down a production site's capacity that should have been reserved for other products.

Beyond the structural records, the data manager governs the change process for all planning parameters. Parameters do not change themselves, and they should not change without review. A planner who believes a supplier's lead time has shortened may ask for the lead time in MDM system to be updated. The data manager's role is not to approve or deny that request on instinct — it is to ensure the request goes through a process: the change is submitted formally, the impact on the supply plan is assessed before the change is applied, the appropriate owner (procurement, in this case) approves it, and once applied, the change is confirmed in the downstream systems and documented. The process exists because a lead time reduction applied without review can cause Planning Software to defer planned orders, reducing safety stock coverage in ways that are not immediately visible but materialise as service failures four to six weeks later.

ERP: Downstream Validation

The data manager's relationship with ERP is not about entering data — it is about confirming that the data they entered in MDM system arrived correctly. This distinction matters because MDM system and ERP are separate systems connected by an integration layer, and integration layers fail, reject records, and introduce mapping errors.

After a change is made in MDM system, the data manager's next task is to verify that ERP received it. Most MDM-to-ERP integrations run on a scheduled basis — typically nightly — so a change applied in MDM system on Tuesday morning will not appear in ERP until Wednesday's sync window has run. The data manager checks the integration log to confirm the change was transmitted and accepted, then validates the affected record in ERP directly: not just that a field exists, but that the value matches what was set in MDM system.

Divergence between MDM system and ERP is a red flag that demands immediate investigation. If MDM system shows a supplier lead time of 12 days and ERP shows 8 days, there are three possible explanations: the integration did not transmit the change (a send failure), ERP received the change but rejected it due to a field validation rule or mapping error (a receive failure), or someone edited ERP directly without going through MDM system (a governance failure). Each explanation has a different fix, and confusing them leads to applying the wrong one.

Direct edits to ERP that bypass MDM system are the governance failure that data managers find most difficult to manage, because they are often well-intentioned. A planner who needs an urgent correction — a minimum order quantity that is causing an unrealistic planned order, for example — may not be willing to wait for a formal MDM change process that takes 48 hours. So they edit ERP directly, the plan corrects itself, and the problem appears solved. What has actually happened is that the next MDM-to-ERP sync will overwrite the direct ERP edit with whatever is in MDM system, reverting the correction and reintroducing the problem. The data manager needs to identify when bypasses have occurred, ensure that both the MDM system record and the ERP record end up correct through the proper process, and then treat the bypass as process feedback: if planners are routinely bypassing MDM system because the change process is too slow for urgent corrections, the process needs a fast-track path for urgent cases, not a policy reminder email.

Planning Software: Monitoring the Downstream Effect

The data manager does not own Planning Software and does not make entries there in normal operation. Their interest in Planning Software is entirely about the downstream effect of what they have done — or failed to do — in MDM system.

After a significant MDM system change, the data manager checks Planning Software to verify two things. First, that the change propagated correctly: the updated parameter values are visible in Planning Software's data, not the old values. Second, that the plan has adjusted in the expected direction. If a supplier lead time was extended from 10 days to 15 days, Planning Software should now be placing planned orders earlier to compensate. If it is not, either the change did not propagate or Planning Software has a data override in place that is masking the MDM system value.

Unexpected plan behaviour after an MDM system change is one of the most important diagnostic signals the data manager will encounter. A BOM restructure that causes Planning Software to generate a flood of exception alerts across products that should not have been affected is not simply an inconvenient output — it is telling the data manager that something went wrong in how the change was applied, or that the change has exposed an existing data quality problem that was previously masked. A lead time correction that causes planned order dates to shift in the wrong direction suggests that another parameter — a reorder point or a safety stock level — is interacting with the lead time change in a way that was not anticipated during the impact assessment. These interactions are the reason the change process requires an impact assessment before application, not after.

The data manager also monitors Planning Software for patterns that suggest systematic data quality problems without a recent change as the trigger. A product family that consistently generates exception alerts for implausibly large planned order quantities is likely suffering from a lot size parameter in MDM system that no longer reflects how the supplier actually operates — perhaps because the supplier revised their minimum pallet configuration two years ago and the MDM system record was never updated. These patterns are subtle and require the data manager to look at Planning Software not just after changes, but as a periodic diagnostic exercise. The plan is, in this sense, an output of the data manager's own work quality, and reading it as such is part of the job.

FMS: An Organisational Dependency

The data manager's involvement with FMS is the most variable aspect of the role, because it depends on how the organisation has structured the relationship between field-side product classification and planning-side item codes.

In organisations where FMS uses the product hierarchy from MDM system to classify grower crops against planning item codes — mapping "Field A's spring wheat harvest" to "Raw Material Item 00421" — the data manager governs that mapping. A wrong mapping means that field supply signals entering the supply chain are attributed to the wrong product in Planning Software. The supply plan for Item 00421 may look adequately covered while the product it is actually receiving against is showing a shortfall, because the signals are arriving under the wrong code. When a new crop variety is introduced or a grower contract changes the product specification, the data manager must update the mapping before the first delivery is received, not after.

In organisations where FMS maintains its own product classification independently of MDM system, the data manager's involvement moves to the integration boundary: the translation layer that maps FMS product codes to MDM system item codes. This layer must be accurate and current. If a code-mapping is stale or missing, FMS data either fails to integrate or integrates against the wrong item, and the consequences for the supply plan are the same as a wrong mapping in the direct-hierarchy case — just harder to trace because the error lives in the translation layer rather than in either system directly.

Why the Cross-System Dimension Is Not Optional

Most data quality problems do not announce themselves in MDM system. They announce themselves in Planning Software or ERP — as a wrong recommendation, an unexpected exception alert, a planner who cannot reconcile what the plan is telling them with what they know about their supply base. The data manager's job, when these symptoms surface, is to receive the report, trace it back to the root cause (which is almost always in MDM system or in the integration from MDM system), apply the correct fix, and confirm that the fix has had the intended effect in every downstream system.

This trace-fix-confirm loop is what makes the role cross-system, and it cannot be done by someone who is only fluent in MDM system. Tracing a Planning Software exception to a yield factor in a BOM requires knowing how Planning Software uses BOM data. Confirming that an ERP divergence was caused by an integration rejection rather than a governance bypass requires knowing what an integration rejection looks like in ERP's log format. The cross-system fluency is not a bonus capability — it is the core of what makes a data manager effective rather than merely active.

For someone coming to the data manager role from a planning or operations background, the shift in perspective is significant. As a planner, a wrong recommendation is something you escalate and work around. As a data manager, that same wrong recommendation is the beginning of your investigation — a signal that something you are responsible for is incorrect, and that it is your job to find it, fix it, and make sure it does not recur.
