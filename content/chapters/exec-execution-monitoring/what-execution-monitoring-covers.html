Monitoring Is Not Planning

Execution monitoring is sometimes conflated with planning — both involve looking at supply, demand, and inventory. The distinction is fundamental. Planning asks: what should happen? Monitoring asks: what is actually happening? Planning works with forecasts, targets, and confirmed schedules. Monitoring works with events, confirmations, and physical movements.

A planner looks at a confirmed production schedule and asks whether it is achievable. An execution monitor looks at a production order and asks whether the scheduled batch has started, whether it is running on time, and whether yield is tracking to expectation. The inputs are different. The questions are different. The time horizon is different — planning typically looks days to weeks ahead; monitoring looks at the current operating period and the next 24 to 48 hours.

Keeping these roles distinct matters because the data they require is different, the system views they use are different, and the decisions they make are different. Conflating them leads to planners spending time on operational fire-fighting they cannot control, and execution teams reading plan views that do not reflect what is actually happening on the floor.

What Execution Monitoring Tracks

Execution monitoring covers four categories of operational activity.

Production throughput — tracking actual production output against the confirmed schedule. For each production order or batch in the current period, monitoring confirms whether the run has started, how actual output compares to the scheduled quantity, and whether any yield loss or quality issue has been flagged. Throughput monitoring is the earliest signal of a supply shortfall within the current horizon.

Shipment status for open outbound orders — tracking each confirmed customer delivery from despatch to proof of delivery. At any point in the operating period, the execution team should be able to say which orders have been picked, which have been loaded, which are in transit, and which have been delivered and confirmed. Shipments not yet confirmed by their required delivery window become priority exceptions.

Inbound receipt status from suppliers — tracking expected purchase orders against confirmed and pending receipts. A supplier who was expected to deliver 500 units today has either delivered, partially delivered, or failed to deliver. Each of those outcomes has a different implication for the day's allocation plan and may require immediate exception handling.

Inventory movements — all adjustments, transfers, and write-offs. Inventory is not a static number between planning runs. Stock moves through the warehouse, is transferred between locations, is scrapped when damaged or expired, and is adjusted when cycle counts reveal discrepancies. Each movement changes the available position, and unrecorded movements create a gap between the system's view of inventory and physical reality.

Why Monitoring Must Be Continuous

Supply chain operations do not pause between planning cycles. Production runs, trucks move, receipts arrive, and customers expect deliveries throughout the operating day. Execution monitoring must be continuous — or near-continuous — because the consequences of an undetected deviation compound quickly. A production batch that misses its window at 8am may require a resequencing decision by 10am to protect afternoon despatch commitments. A supplier short-shipment identified at 9am can be partially mitigated by afternoon; identified at 4pm, the options are much narrower.

Monitoring is not a passive activity. It requires active attention to the exception queue, prompt confirmation of completed events, and rapid escalation when deviations occur that cannot be absorbed within the current plan.
