What an escalation path is

An escalation path is the predefined route that a planning issue travels from discovery to resolution. It answers: when a planner cannot resolve an issue within their authority, who do they contact, with what information, and by when?

The absence of clear escalation paths creates two failure modes. The first is under-escalation: issues that require a decision are held by a planner who lacks the authority or information to resolve them — they sit in the queue until they become crises. The second is over-escalation: every small deviation goes to senior management because no one knows what can be handled at which level — leaders spend their time on operational noise and miss the strategic issues.

Common escalation triggers in planning

Issues typically escalate when they:

  • Exceed authority limits — the planner's decision framework allows adjustments within parameters; outside those parameters, escalation is required
  • Have customer-facing consequences — a projected service level miss, a pending order delay, or an allocation decision that affects a key customer
  • Require cross-functional decisions — resolving a supply shortage may require a commercial decision (which customer loses supply), a financial decision (does it make sense to expedite?), and an operations decision (can capacity be flexed?)
  • Cannot be resolved within S&OE — the weekly S&OE meeting is the first escalation tier; issues that cannot be resolved there escalate to leadership

Designing the escalation path

A practical escalation path has three elements:

Trigger — the condition that initiates escalation. Ideally this is objective: "any projected stock-out within four weeks for an A-class product," not "when the planner feels it's serious enough."

Route — who the issue goes to, in what format, and with what supporting information. The recipient needs enough context to make a decision without needing to re-investigate.

Timeline — by when a response is required. An escalation without a response deadline is a request, not an escalation. The timeline should be tied to the planning horizon: an issue that becomes irreversible in five days needs a response in one or two days.

Escalation and the planning cadence

Escalation paths should be integrated with the planning cadence. S&OE is the standard escalation forum for weekly operational issues. Pre-S&OP is the standard escalation forum for month-horizon gaps. Executive S&OP is the escalation forum for strategic issues and unresolved pre-S&OP items. Escalations outside the cadence (ad hoc executive requests, emergency customer calls) should be the exception, not the norm — and when they occur, they should feed back into the standard process so the root cause is addressed.
